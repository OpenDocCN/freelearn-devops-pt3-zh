Introducing the VSM Implementation Roadmap

Adopting Value Stream Management is not just a step, but a journey towards continuous improvement to create an ever-evolving insights-driven culture.

– Adapted from the VSM Consortium

Previously, in Chapter 4, we introduced VSM as a methodology for prioritizing opportunities using a Lean approach. This chapter explores the VSM Implementation Roadmap from the Value Stream Management Consortium (VSMC)1. The VSMC promotes value stream methods and tools for Lean-Agile enterprises, particularly in organizations heavily invested in digital technologies. They facilitate optimizing strategic investments and end-to-end operations, avoiding siloed decision-making. This road map enables the widespread deployment of the VSM methodology throughout your organization, leveraging both Lean and Agile concepts while aligning the technology departments to support the organization’s value stream improvement activities.

The VSM Implementation Roadmap comprises the following steps: start, assess, vision, identify, organize, map, connect, inspect, and adapt. It combines Lean and Agile practices, aligned with the fundamental principles of our book — The Lean-Agile Way.

The VSM implementation roadmap goes across industry boundaries, ensuring holistic, end-to- end value stream assessments essential for achieving significant flow improvements and avoiding local optimizations, common when systems thinking is ignored.

Mastery of the VSM implementation roadmap enables your organization to optimize value streams systematically, understand their complexities, and implement transformative change within your enterprise.

Unveiling the VSM implementation roadmap

Before explaining the purpose and the essential steps of the VSM implementation roadmap, it’s helpful to understand VSM’s history, which has paved the way for this modern approach to implementation.

Learning from history

The concept of value streams has a substantial historical background, with numerous contributors influencing its development. While we have come a long way, this history remains relevant as it helps us understand the necessity of today’s VSM implementation roadmap. So, before we go on, let’s take a look at some of the most impactful historical events in the evolution of Lean thinking and practices.

  • Toyota’s early use: Toyota is credited with the early implementation of value stream concepts, using visual displays of production processes, known as material and information flow diagrams. However, controversy exists about when these concepts and tools were initially used at Toyota (Baudin 2013).2
  • Womack and Jones: James Womack and Daniel Jones introduced the term “value stream” in the ‘From Lean Production to the Lean Enterprise’ Harvard Business Review article.3 It emphasized that value streams define the Lean enterprise and encompass all activities needed to deliver maximum customer value.
  • James Martin’s insight: James Martin, a highly respected figure in the computer industry, defined value streams in the context of a holistic practice he called ‘Enterprise Engineering’ (Martin 1994).4 Martin advocated against automating poorly designed business processes mapped to hierarchical management structures, which invariably cause siloes and inefficiencies across functional and organizational workstreams.
  • Emergence of VSM books: In the early 2000s, books on VSM began to appear, with ‘Value Stream Management: Strategy and Excellence in the Supply Chain’ (Hines et al. 2000) as one of the earliest.5 This book described VSM as practices that enhance customer experiences and listed seven value stream mapping tools.
  • Refining versus mapping: Subsequent books, including ‘Learning to See’ by Mike Rother and John Shook and ‘Seeing the Whole Value Stream’ by David Brunt, Jim Womack, Dan Jones, and Matthew Lovejoy, played a significant role in refining value stream mapping techniques. 6,7 These publications began incorporating Lean metrics to facilitate the discovery of constraints and waste affecting an organization’s end-to-end value stream flows.
  • VSM as a methodology: Books by Don Tapping and his co-authors further refined VSM as a methodology for continuous improvement using Lean principles and value stream mapping.8
  • Widespread adoption: Over time, the application of value stream improvement concepts expanded to various industries. Karen Martin and Mike Osterling’s book, ‘Value Stream Mapping: How to Visualize Work and Align Leadership for Organizational Transformation’ (Martin, Osterling 2013) significantly popularized these ideas.9
  • Scope and internal value streams: Martin and Osterling further emphasized setting boundaries and limitations in value stream mapping to enable organizations to stay focused and promoted the idea of support-oriented value streams to map processes for internal activities such as HR, IT, Finance, and Sales.

Several other important contributors have or are continuing to evolve modern VSM practices. However, this historical perspective offers a sufficient background to understand why modern VSM practices must incorporate Lean-Agile concepts and the primary reason for creating the VSM Implementation Roadmap. Also, the Scaled Agile Framework has been a significant contributor to popularizing value stream concepts outside of the manufacturing domain and within agile frameworks.

Examining the need for a VSM roadmap

What we have learned so far is that value streams embody a Lean concept that has evolved to address issues associated with the conventional practice of managing business processes within isolated silos designed to accommodate hierarchical organizational structures. Too often, these hierarchies have resulted in gross inefficiencies, political infighting for people, funding, and other resources, lack of focus on customer-centricity, and an inability to evaluate investments holistically, let alone from a customer-centric point of view.

In contrast, value streams are centered on comprehending how organizations generate value from their customers’ perspectives, transcending organizational boundaries and self-interests. In essence, value streams define a seamless flow of work, materials, information, and resources that efficiently deliver value without being confined by organizational boundaries. The aim of value streams is to eliminate waste and constraints that often lead to product delays, increased costs, and decreased quality.

In Chapter 4, we explored how VSM emerged as a methodology designed to assist organizations in assessing their most critical improvements to value flow. As a methodology, VSM follows this generalized pattern:

  • Teaching Lean-Agile concepts
  • Identifying and prioritizing value streams
  • Defining an idealized value state for benchmarking
  • Utilizing current state value stream mapping through Gemba walks to uncover waste and constraints
  • Visualizing proposed value stream improvements through future state value streams
  • Developing Kaizen improvement plans that document recommended changes, including assessments of the current state , desired future state, gap analysis, ROI justifications, prioritization, and alternative recommendations.
  • Executing the Kaizen plan to achieve defined goals and metrics
  • Establishing a culture of continuous improvement and transformation
  • Stopping the continuous improvement process for a product or service when it reaches the end of its economically viable life cycle

This comprehensive approach ensures that organizations identify improvement opportunities and execute and sustain these improvements as an integral part of their transformative business culture. However, with the advent of Agile methodologies, it becomes essential to rethink how the VSM methodology should integrate Lean and Agile practices. That is the subject of the following section.

Exploring the VSM Implementation Roadmap

Embracing the Lean-Agile mindset, the VSMC’s approach to VSM is designed to enhance value delivery through continuous improvement and customer focus.

Figure 5.1 illustrates the VSM implementation roadmap, which is a series of high-level steps guiding an organization adopting VSM ways of working. Each step establishes a foundation for the next, contributing to the evolution of an organization that practices VSM-led approaches, continually optimizing the customer experience, and illustrates the VSMC implementation roadmap, which is integral to the VSM methodology. While identifying and prioritizing continuous improvement, the VSM roadmap integrates inspect and adapt concepts from Agile. Inspecting and adapting enables continuous progress review and adaptation strategies based on new learning and market shifts. Each step of the roadmap brings us toward excellence in customer value delivery and satisfaction. It consists of the following steps:

Figure 5.1 – VSM Implementation Roadmap graphic

Although the roadmap presents the implementation steps as a linear flow, in reality, each step may occur iteratively based on feedback and learning. The list that follows identifies the purpose of each step.

  1. Start: Initiate VSM by setting the step for change and commitment, aligning the mission with Lean-Agile principles.
  2. Assess: Evaluate the current operational landscape (culture, capability, and context), baseline current performance, and establish a business case for improvement.
  3. Vision: A compelling vision for the future inspires and motivates people to act, establishing a clear and strategic direction for value creation and visualizing the outcomes that will drive success.
  4. Identify: Select a value stream to improve. Focus on customer value to identify the organization’s value streams. This can be challenging for project-centric organizations, as long-lived products, unlike short-lived projects, reveal value streams, with anything delivering a product or service constituting a value stream.

    The first value stream to target for improvement is often the one that lies at the intersection of four criteria: strong leadership support, highly visible value stream, existing Agile teams, and a significant challenge or opportunity.

  5. Organize: Systematically organize people, resources, and workflows around a product or service to optimize the flow of value for a product or service across functional departments, and the entire system.
  6. Map: Assemble the VSM team, to identify and understand the current steps and activities involved in delivering value to the customer. Mapping helps identify bottlenecks, delays, and areas for improvement in the value stream.
  7. Connect: Integrate and connect people, processes, and tools within value streams that foster communication and teamwork. When work items can be traced throughout the entire value stream, delays, rework, and waste become visible. These insights drive improvements, and feedback informs decision-making.
  8. Inspect: Monitor performance by analyzing data, setting improvement goals, defining critical metrics, and leveraging automated data collection systems for accurate tracking. Continuous inspection, management, and improvement of flow and value realization are feasible only by making data and insights available from the digital value stream.
  9. Adapt: Once you have data and insights to inform action, it’s time to implement changes. Encourage continuous, iterative refinements to adapt to change and proactively strive for excellence.

By adhering to its structured approach, the VSMC roadmap streamlines operations and persistently aligns them with the overarching goal of delivering superior value to our customers. Each step offers a commitment to the Lean-Agile mindset, ensuring enterprises stay Agile, responsive, and customer- centric in our journey toward operational excellence.

We will further explore each roadmap step.

Implementing Value Stream Management

According to the VSMC, implementing VSM goes beyond operational improvements. It embodies efficiency, responsiveness, and strategic foresight. Consequently, this perspective is foundational for appreciating the nuances of their VSM implementation roadmap. The following sections provide a detailed overview of the nine roadmap steps.

1. Start – initiating value stream management

Your VSM implementation journey begins with your current state, regardless of the maturity of your digital processes. At the core of each business are value streams and networks of value streams designed to fulfill customer needs or serve citizens (in government contexts). Organizations continuously innovate, develop, and deliver products or services regardless of their profit orientation.

Whenever there is a spark of innovation or a proposed change, it goes through design, development, and delivery before the customer can realize its value. Many organizations have long since adopted Agile methodologies, DevOps practices, or Lean principles. Others might be navigating through structural changes such as reorganizations, mergers, or acquisitions. These factors do not prevent enterprises from adopting and driving the use of VSM methodologies and tools. Instead they strengthen the business case for change. However, leaders must be wary of the fatigue that comes with constant change. Clearly articulate the purpose and vision of the VSM implementation and help knowledge workers understand how their work contributes to the organization’s overall goals and objectives. A good purpose offers the why behind the change and motivates people to come along on the journey and to be actively engaged in its success.

The typical obstacles to adopting VSM are the absence of senior leadership endorsement and limited resources. Forward-thinking individuals should proceed with the intent to course-correct rather than wait for permission, bringing leaders along in the process. Proactive individuals recognize that perceived resource limitations are often alleviated when inefficiencies are eliminated, unlocking capacity within the system. Still, this proactive stance does not abdicate executives from their duties in transforming the organization.

Jumping in front of a parade does not make you a leader. True leadership involves more than just being at the forefront. It requires vision, empathy, and the ability to inspire and guide others toward a common goal. A true leader listens, collaborates, and leads by example rather than merely seeking the spotlight.

Leaders earn the credibility and authority to steer the company forward through a hands-on approach and intimate knowledge of the organization’s inner workings. This involvement goes beyond leading change to being an integral part of the transformation, ensuring that the implementation of VSM is a shared journey.

While emphasizing the importance of leadership involvement, we do not advocate for a scenario where leaders direct all tasks or make every decision single-handedly. Such centralization can lead to detrimental outcomes, including decision-making bottlenecks, uninformed choices, and biases that contradict the actual dynamics of the organization.

Effective leaders understand the value of delegation and place trust in their teams to handle the tactical, day-to-day decisions. Handing off decision-making is easier when the organization makes visible activities and critical metrics that define progress against goals and plans. This approach empowers employees, fosters a culture of accountability, and ensures that the organization’s direction is shaped by diverse insights and expertise, leading to a more robust and resilient VSM implementation.

Figure 5.2 – VSM starting activities

These key activities lay the foundation and set the direction for effective VSM implementation:

  • Assemble core teams: Creating a foundational team for VSM should bring together individuals with a wide range of expertise. Two types of teams are recommended:
    • VSM SME team – A small, specialized group of VSM subject matter experts (SMEs) who facilitate, educate, and mentor throughout the organization’s various VSM initiatives. They are responsible for driving the VSM methodology and overseeing the implementation of VSMC roadmap activities.
    • Value stream improvements team (VSIT) – This team has individuals actively involved in enhancing the targeted value streams. They possess critical domain knowledge and play a vital role in implementing the concepts later outlined in Chapter 9, Defining a Business Agility System for the Enterprise (BASE), including establishing a rhythm to support the objectives of the minimum valuable increment (MVI). The composition of the VSIT is dynamic and may vary with each MVI.
  • Assess the current situation: This step requires an initial assessment of the current landscape. Immediate executive support is ideal, but if that’s not available, the goal is to generate momentum and gather the support and resources needed for the VSM journey.
  • Set strategic direction: The focus here is to outline the strategic goals and objectives of the VSM initiative. This means considering how it supports the company’s broader strategic goals, enhances customer value, improves operational efficiency, and boosts overall business performance.

These three ‘start’ activities offer several benefits to organizations. First, they establish the core resources necessary to kickstart a successful VSM initiative by forming specialized teams. Secondly, they provide a rapid assessment of the organization’s current status, bridging the gap between its existing state and the strategic goals required to achieve the broader business strategies. Another benefit it provides is the alignment and direction needed to enable decentralized decision-making and execution, tapping into the collective intelligence of teams.

With these three critical activities completed, the VSM and VSIT teams embark upon the second VSM step, ‘assess.’

2. Assess – evaluating and baselining current performance

Assess, the second step in the VSM implementation roadmap, builds upon the ‘start’ step, and involves a comprehensive evaluation of existing VSM capabilities to establish baseline performance metrics and identify areas for improvement.

The VSMC developed a VSM assessment that is aligned with the implementation roadmap steps. It is designed to baseline the current state and for ongoing reassessment during your VSM implementation journey. This assessment phase emerged from collaborative efforts within the VSMC, underscoring the importance of benchmarking the current state at the beginning of the VSM journey and maintaining continuous progress monitoring.

The VSMC’s assessment tool utilizes a Likert scale questionnaire, as shown in Figure 5.3, prompting respondents to rate their current state on a scale ranging from Strongly agree to Strongly disagree and Not applicable.

Figure 5.3 – Likert scale assessment

Tailored for use by consultants and enterprise practitioners, this assessment tool is valuable for identifying focus areas, establishing a baseline for future measurement, and comparing performance against industry benchmarks.

Available online as a self-service tool, the assessment invites teams to respond to standardized questions – VSM Assessment (vsmconsortium.org/vsm-assessment). This tool aids in internal benchmarking and allows for a comparison with other teams and organizations. It starts with evaluating the organization’s vision and leadership support – key drivers of successful VSM adoption.

By leveraging this tool, teams can systematically uncover strengths and weaknesses in their approach, providing a clear, quantifiable measure of their VSM journey and identifying a path forward to elevate their value stream practices to world-class standards.

Having completed the thorough ‘assess’ phase, where current processes are evaluated, we now transition to the ‘vision’ step. Here, we shift our focus from understanding the current state of our value stream to defining our desired future state vision.

3. Vision – creating a unified vision and goals

In the vision step, organizations must define and articulate a long-term vision and objectives that guide their transformation journey. This vision must be well understood and broadly shared across the organization, becoming the new norm. Put simply, this is the way we do things around here. It’s not merely about fighting through existing systems, but evolving the organizational mindset and instilling new habits. Figure 5.4 depicts the essential elements of the vision step:

Figure 5.4 – Developing a shared vision

The vision reinforces an organization’s dedication to defining the seamless flow of valuable customer outcomes. It is a process where the organization envisions a future free from waste and unnecessary constraints, gains a deep understanding of customer experiences, and promptly acts on them, with each insight driving tangible improvements.

The goals associated with this vision are inherently united with enhancing organizational effectiveness, especially critical in the digital for-profit sphere. These goals encompass high responsiveness to customer needs, transparency of work in process, empowering value stream autonomy, and achieving a competitive edge in the market. Measured improvement forms the foundation for these objectives, enabling tracking and optimizing delivery speed, customer impact, cost efficiency, and market leadership.

The goals of the vision step are designed to elevate organizational effectiveness by emphasizing agility, transparency, and adaptability to market dynamics. The objective is to promptly address customer needs, ensure clear and effective processes, and enable value streams to directly influence financial success. Goals must be aspirational and measurable using leading indicators. The SMART criteria discussed in Chapter 4 offer a structured approach for tracking business objectives and metrics.

Leading indicators enable tracking an organization’s proficiency in enacting changes swiftly, efficiently meeting customer demands, managing costs effectively, and sustaining competitive advantage within its market.

Executive leaders must embrace their role of setting the strategic course and empowering teams with strategic insight to actively engage in the transformation. It is a collaborative effort where goals are set, such as improving customer experience, reducing process waste, shortening feedback loops, and aligning with financial models. In Chapter 9, Defining a Business Agility System for the Enterprise (BASE), we’ll discuss the use of OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) to help executives set clear expectations.

Each objective is associated with quantifiable outcomes such as increased customer engagement, faster delivery of value, improved decision-making, and positive financial returns. By breaking down the vision into measurable targets, teams can pursue incremental progress, making the vision a tangible and achievable reality.

Having a clear vision lays the groundwork for transforming aspirations into measurable outcomes, propelling the organization forward on its VSM journey with a shared direction.

4. Identify – surfacing value streams

The goal of the identify step is to define and describe the value streams within an organization. Seeing value streams in a landscape often dominated by hierarchal organizational structures and project-based thinking can present challenges. Unlike projects, which are temporary and finite, value streams represent the life cycle of a product or service, from inception to delivery and beyond. They are the sequences of activities required to transform an idea into a valuable outcome for the customer.

To illustrate, in the context of an e-commerce system for selling products, core value streams include the activities most critical to the mission of the organization, while supportive value streams include the platforms and CI/CD pipelines facilitating core value stream activities. We go further into classification methods for types of streams in Part 3.

The identification process involves recognizing various products, services, platforms, and their respective internal or external customers. Each value stream has a starting point where an idea is generated and an endpoint where value is delivered to the customer. It is essential to map these value streams, understand where they intersect and support one another, and identify dependencies.

The goal is to take incremental steps toward improvement. Organizations can systematically unravel the complexities of their value streams by beginning with small-scale experiments and learning from them. The primary focus is on identifying and naming value-adding steps, identifying the customers they serve, and mapping out the entire journey from start to finish. Adopting this approach allows even the most intricate systems to be effectively navigated and optimized over time.

You may notice that the VSM implementation roadmap has Lean and Agile elements. In this context, we utilize the iterative and incremental development process from Agile to identify opportunities for Lean-oriented improvements within the organization. During the ‘inspect’ and ‘adapt’ phases, we’ll revisit the concepts of iteration and incremental improvement. However, we still have more ground to cover as we move on to the next step – ‘organize.’

5. Organize – streamlining cross-functional collaboration

The organize step acknowledges the shift from traditional siloed structures to a more integrated approach where technology teams are intrinsically aligned with the business. The evolution from DevOps to a broader inclusion of departments such as Finance, HR, Sales, Marketing, Product Management, and Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) highlights this shift.

The problems inherent in silos are significant. Handoffs between teams present risks of errors, delays, or lost opportunities. Moreover, organizational layers and disconnects can lead to misalignment with business intentions, resulting in slower delivery and reduced customer satisfaction.

Therefore, core value streams must be seen as spanning the entire journey from concept to customer realization without artificially separating business and technology. Certain roles, such as portfolio managers, may not be dedicated to single value streams due to their overarching nature. Similarly, specialized roles such as release and cloud engineers may operate across multiple streams, possibly within a platform or enabling teams.

Many people, both internal and external to the organization, may care about the products and services we deliver. However, the ultimate stakeholder is the customer, and value streams should operate autonomously to serve them.

Although roles are situated at specific points within the value chain, all roles should focus on the flow and realization of value within their respective streams rather than their mere contribution to the process.

The following considerations are vital to the ‘organize’ step:

  • Identification of VSM and cross-functional teams: Recognize the necessary roles and form teams that can operate across functional boundaries to improve the value stream
  • Definition of roles and responsibilities: Delineate what each team and its members are accountable for, ensuring that every role is geared toward enhancing the value provided to the customer
  • Communication: Establish robust communication that facilitates seamless interaction and information sharing across all teams
  • Training and support: Provide comprehensive training and resources to teams, enabling them to fulfill their VSM roles effectively
  • Monitoring and adjustment: Continuously monitor the performance of the value stream, ready to adjust team organization and dynamics to ensure optimal functioning and value delivery

By embracing these principles, organizations can create a cohesive environment that promotes efficiency, alignment, and customer-centricity, moving away from siloed operations toward a fully integrated digital value stream.

We are poised to move forward with the VSM teams in place to facilitate training, coaching, and mentoring of the cross-functional VSIT teams assembled. Step 6, ‘map,’ will examine the value stream mapping process, which equips teams to pinpoint areas for enhancement.

6. Map – visualizing process efficiency and opportunities

Building a value stream map is a key step that offers a structured approach to analyzing and improving the flow of value. The process typically consists of the following steps:

  • Assemble your mapping team: This initial step involves gathering a cross-functional team whose members were identified earlier during the Organize step. This team will work collaboratively to create the value stream map, bringing together their diverse expertise and perspectives.
  • Define the scope: The scope of the mapping exercise must be clearly defined, establishing the boundaries of the value stream to be mapped. This includes setting the starting and ending points, typically from the initial customer request to the final product delivery.
  • Create the current state map: Working backward from value delivery to the initial customer request, the team visually represents the value stream’s current state, composed of each distinct process in the flow. This map serves as a baseline for identifying waste, bottlenecks, and areas for potential improvement.
  • Gather process data: In this phase, the team collects relevant data for each process within the value stream. Important metrics include cycle times, value-added time to the initial customer request, work in process, wait times, and other metrics to understand the flow and delays.
  • Analyze the map: The final step thoroughly analyzes the current state map. Team members are encouraged to share insights and perspectives on potential changes or optimizations. This collaborative review is designed to generate a shared understanding of the value stream and foster consensus on the next steps for improvement.

Through these steps, the mapping process captures the current state of the value stream and serves as a springboard for continuous improvement. By involving a cross-functional team, the mapping exercise ensures consideration of all perspectives, leading to a more comprehensive and effective approach to improvement.

7. Connect – orchestrating people, processes, and tools

Given our imperative to compete in the digital era, connect is pivotal for integrating the DevOps toolchain into the value stream. The toolchain spans the entire life cycle of an idea, beginning with portfolio management and progressing through the product backlog, continuous integration/ continuous deployment (CI/CD), release and environment management, and finally, the service desk. This comprehensive suite of tools is integral for tracking an idea from its origin to the realized value as perceived by the customer, allowing for a deep analysis of the customer experience.

The ‘connect’ activities are designed to create a seamless and efficient workflow by integrating communication, technology, and feedback. Modern VSM tools and platforms provide the integration, visualization, and analysis capabilities to support the connect phase.

The following provides a brief overview of the connect activities:

  • Define communication protocols: This activity involves establishing clear guidelines on how team members exchange information, ensuring that communication is consistent and effective across all value stream levels.
  • Integrate technology and tools: This step combines various technology solutions and tools into a cohesive system that supports the value stream, enhancing the team’s ability to work cohesively and efficiently.
  • Automate data collection: This activity aims to reduce manual effort, minimize errors, and provide a consistent and reliable data flow for analysis and decision-making.
  • Enable real-time visibility: This involves setting up systems that provide immediate insight into the value stream’s performance, allowing for prompt identification of issues and opportunities for improvement.
  • Establish feedback loops: Creating feedback loops supports continuous learning and improvement, enabling the value stream to adapt and evolve based on the insights gathered from the team’s experiences and the data collected.

Each of these activities plays a vital role in connecting the various elements of the digital value stream, ensuring that the process is as efficient and responsive as possible.

Tracing work items throughout the entire value stream is a powerful capability provided by the tools shown in Figure 5.6. They illuminate delays, rework, and any waste that occurs along the way. The insights gathered can inform improvements by making these elements visible, while the feedback drives informed decision-making.

In summary, connecting people, processes, and tools creates a cohesive, transparent workflow where every action and outcome can be traced back to its source. It ensures the team is aligned, learning from real-time data, and continuously adapting to optimize the value stream. This connection phase is not just a step but an ongoing practice that underpins a culture of efficiency and continuous improvement.

8. Inspect – using the Plan Do Study Act (PDSA) cycle to improve value streams

Value streams originated with Lean, but W. Edwards Deming’s Plan Do Study Act (PDSA) guidance provided a significant foundation. So, it shouldn’t be surprising that we recommend using the PDSA cycle he popularized to improve our understanding of value streams.

PDSA goes beyond simple “inspect and adapt.” It uses evidence-based decision-making, which avoids speculation in favor of empiricism while using validated theory. This process takes us ahead of preconceived notions found in traditional methods.

  • Plan: Before we do something, we need a hypothesis and a plan. The timeframe for planning can vary from minutes to days. The plan sets an expectation of what we think will happen. This tests our understanding of the situation and how well it works. It provides a baseline to see how well we understood the current situation.
  • Do: This is where we execute the plan’s activities. However, if evidence proves things are not going as expected, we refine the plan to reflect on our new understanding of the situation. The Deming PDSA cycle applies continuous learning for a planned timebox.
  • Study: We use fast feedback to determine whether what is happening (or “Do”) matches what we expected (our “Plan”). This feedback loop can be continuous or conducted at the end of the planned increment. Looking at the disparity between plan and do, we can pivot and challenge our understanding. In other words, we study to ensure we are building the right product in the right way while making sure we are also thinking correctly.

    This process is called double-loop learning. Single-loop learning is about getting better at what you are doing. Double loop learning challenges the “what you are doing” to see whether there is a better way to work and whether what you’re working on is correct. Any process that doesn’t allow you to change its practices effectively limits double-loop learning.

    Triple-loop learning is about learning how to learn. Triple-loop learning teaches us how to challenge our assumptions. The article on double-loop learning by Chris Argyris at https://infed.org/mobi/chris-argyris-theories-of-action-double-loop-learning-and-organizational-learning/ offers greater detail on this topic.

  • Act: After the “Study” phase, our focus turns to producing what we are building by following our plan while improving our understanding of the model we used to develop the plan.

The VSMC advocates for inspecting value along two principal dimensions:

  • Flow: Ensuring the work travels smoothly from conception to delivery, representing a consistent delivery of value to customers
  • Value: Actualization of the desired business outcomes, where customers experience the intended value

These dimensions help the organization assess the health of the value stream and the customer experience to assess efficiency and effectiveness.

The ‘inspect’ phase allows us to focus on analyzing and enhancing the performance of value streams. This involves a series of strategic actions aimed at understanding and improving the various facets of the value stream processes. Here are the key activities that drive this phase:

  • Define relevant metrics: Define essential performance indicators for your value streams, such as lead time, cycle time, throughput, ongoing work, and defect rates.
  • Leverage automated data collection: Use real-time data from DevOps tools to analyze performance and understand the true progress of work. For example, developers mark work as ‘Done’ in a Kanban system, but the completed work from the story may sit in another team’s release queue for months. DevOps tools and data present a more accurate view, basing metrics on the end-to-end process.
  • Monitor performance: Keep a constant watch on your value stream’s performance by analyzing real-time data and insights from DevOps tools to prevent service disruptions. Teams must continuously monitor system attributes such as security, reliability, performance, scalability, and more.
  • Analyze the data: Examine the data collected to assess how well your processes work and spot areas needing refinement, including business outcomes for products and services.
  • Set experimental improvement goals: Craft targeted goals for experimental improvements to boost your value stream performance based on thorough data analysis.

Maintaining a smooth flow of work and achieving desired business outcomes necessitates a vigilant and proactive approach to management and refinement. The wealth of data and insights in the digital value stream provides the mechanisms that teams need to sustain a perpetual cycle of inspection and adaptation.

9. Adapt – communicate, scope, and plan interventions

The Adapt phase represents the culmination of the VSMC’s VSM implementation roadmap, where insights and data converge to prompt actionable change. Here, the focus shifts to implementing and iterating on changes that emerge from the Inspect phase. This phase is characterized by seven key actions, each aimed at evolving and refining the value stream:

  • Embrace continuous improvement: Commit to relentless improvement, always seeking ways to enhance the value stream
  • Eliminate waste: Identify and remove inefficiencies to streamline processes and maximize value
  • Measure results and iterate: Evaluate the outcomes of changes and make iterative adjustments to ensure continuous progress
  • Fine-tune processes: Adjust and optimize existing procedures for greater effectiveness and efficiency
  • Automate workflows: Implement automation to reduce manual effort and increase consistency and speed
  • Share good practices: Cultivate and apply successful patterns and techniques across the enterprise’s value streams.
  • Foster a culture of adaptation: Nurture an environment where flexibility and change are embraced across the organization to drive improvement

These adapt activities are not isolated efforts. They represent a cohesive approach to adaptation that is integral to the VSM life cycle. The inspect and adapt steps are intrinsically connected, feeding into each other in a continuous loop that reflects the Agile principles of iterative and incremental development. Through this agile-based approach, collaborating teams ensure the value streams are optimized, resilient, and responsive to change.

The ‘adapt’ stage is also about leveraging your value stream map as a communication tool to outline, scope, and coordinate the necessary changes. It is a strategic phase where you formulate and document hypotheses on the potential impact of changes and develop a clear, structured roadmap detailing immediate actions, next steps, and future considerations.

It is essential to acknowledge that each change in the value chain carries the potential for unintended consequences. A value stream does not operate in isolation; a systems-thinking approach is indispensable for a comprehensive understanding and effective management.

As change occurs, the value stream map becomes a living document, highlighting enhancements and redefining the current state. This is where the iterative nature of Agile adaptation shines, providing continuous improvement and a perpetual cycle of development and enhancement.

In our digital era, the VSM inspect step helps VSIT teams find opportunities to use digital advancements to improve areas that challenge the organization’s usual methods. Once insights and data are gathered, it is crucial to implement changes, which is the main focus of the VSM adapt step.

Value stream mapping can draw attention to improvements and redefine the current state. It is essential to recognize that once actions are taken, the map should evolve to avoid future misunderstandings and misalignment.

Comparing VSM methodology and the VSM implementation roadmap

At first glance, the E9 VSM methodology and the VSMC’s implementation roadmap appear to have similar processes, goals, and objectives. They both have nine steps, and some have similar names. But their areas of focus differ. Figure 5.8 and Figure 5.9 compares the two and illustrates the critical differences we will explore in this section:

The VSM methodology has a history of over twenty years, and in our E9 VSM model, comprises a structured set of nine steps, as shown in Figure 5.8:

Figure 5.8 – The VSM methodology

The VSMC’s implementation roadmap is almost three years old and coincidently comprises a distinct set of nine steps, as described in this chapter and shown in Figure 5.9:

Figure 5.9 – VSMC implementation roadmap

While both roadmaps share similarities in processes, goals, and objectives, critical differences exist. The VSM Methodology is a proven Lean-oriented approach to drive improvements in productivity, efficiencies, and quality in any type of value stream. In contrast, the design of the VSMC implementation roadmap modernizes the VSM approach to support competitiveness and provide value-added services in the digital age. Moreover, the VSMC’s roadmap instills Agile’s iterative concepts for inspection and adaptation.

It is not a matter of choosing one approach over the other. Both serve the purpose of enhancing value streams, but they have distinct roles:

  • The VSM methodology assists organizations in identifying and prioritizing value stream improvements that span organizational boundaries. Specifically, it enables collaborating teams to assess and evaluate cross-functional and cross- departmental change opportunities, ensuring that improvements deliver the most impactful customer-centric value with the available resources.
  • The VSM implementation roadmap incorporates Agile’s inspect and adapt steps to improve agility. This roadmap also aligns the activities of the IT organization, driving digital enhancements across the organization’s products and processes. It focuses on creating IT infrastructure to support digital improvements and enhance competitiveness.

Together, these approaches complement each other, ensuring that the value stream improvement teams effectively address the broader organizational improvements and the IT-specific digital enhancements.

While IT organizations can improve their development and delivery services in isolation, they must align with the broader organization’s improvement needs. The VSM roadmap assists in identifying areas where digital enhancements are vital, ensuring that IT effectively supports the organization’s end-to-end value streams for core products and services.

Answers

  1. The three crucial actions involved in step 1, “Start” step are assembling core teams, assessing the current landscape, and setting strategic direction, which together establish foundational resources, provide a rapid organizational assessment, and align VSM initiatives with broader strategic goals.
  2. The Comparative Agility VSM Assessment tool used in step 2, “Assess” facilitates a comprehensive evaluation of an organization’s current VSM capabilities, enabling benchmarking, identifying improvement areas, and guiding teams toward optimizing their value stream practices.
  3. The primary objective of step 3, “Vision” step is to define a long-term vision and objectives that evolve the organizational mindset, ingraining new habits and aligning with measurable goals to enhance effectiveness and competitive edge in the digital, for-profit sphere.
  4. In the context of step 4, “Identify” Core value streams directly deliver an organization’s core products and services, while supportive value streams enable and support these core functions, such as development platforms or customer relationship management systems.
  5. Effective implementation of VSM step 5, “Organize” requires identifying VSM and cross- functional teams, defining roles and responsibilities, establishing communication pathways, providing training and support, and continuously monitoring and adjusting for optimal value stream functioning and delivery.
  6. In step 6, “Map,” the five steps involved in the value stream mapping process are assembling the mapping team, defining the scope, gathering process data, creating the current state map, and analyzing the map to identify improvement opportunities.
  7. The primary activities of step 7, “Connect,” include defining communication protocols, integrating technology and tools, automating data collection, enabling real-time visibility, and establishing feedback loops to create a seamless, efficient, and responsive digital value stream workflow.
  8. The three pillars of empiricism in step 8, “Inspect” are transparency, inspection, and adaptation, contributing to value stream performance enhancement by promoting trust, encouraging continuous feedback, and guiding iterative improvements based on factual and evidence-based decision-making.
  9. The seven key actions of step 9, “Adapt” are measuring results and iterating, embracing continuous improvement, eliminating waste, fine-tuning processes, automating workflows, sharing good practices, and fostering a culture of adaptation to evolve and refine the value stream.
  10. The key differences between the VSM methodology and the VSMC’s Implementation Roadmap are the modernized approach of the latter to enhance competitiveness in the digital age. The VSMC’s roadmap incorporates Lean and Agile concepts, and focuses on IT infrastructure and digital enhancements. The VSM methodology remains valuable for broad, organizational improvements and cross-departmental changes.
