Navigating Value Stream Optimization

The pessimist complains about the wind; the optimist expects it to change; the realist adjusts the sails.

- William Arthur Ward

In the fast-paced world of business management, one key element often separates successful leaders from the rest: the ability to navigate and optimize the flow of work and deliver value within an organization. For example, imagine your organization is like a well-designed ship with a motivated crew and you have a clear destination—delivering value to your customers. Everything seems perfect, but as we all know, the currents and winds of business are not always predictable.

This chapter will explore how to shift from managing projects to products while leveraging digital enhancements. Sticking with our previous nautical metaphor, we’ll illustrate how navigating your value stream’s path is like sailing through shifting winds and flows.

Value streams constantly change; nothing remains stagnant. However, two shifts remain constant in today’s landscape to become a Lean enterprise:

  1. Shifting from projects to products: To remain competitive and customer-centric, we must change our focus from managing short-term projects to continuously evolving our business processes, products, and services across their economically viable life cycles
  2. Harnessing digital enhancements: Employing digital enhancements is key to effectively improving processes, products, and services

It’s vital to introduce value stream operating models to build a solid foundation for adaptation, offering a holistic view of value creation and delivery across the organization. These new operating models integrate people, processes, information, and technology to optimize workflow. The shift from project to product and embracing customer-centricity enables sustainable value creation and delivery.

By understanding how optimizing value streams drives organizational transformation, you can improve solutions for customer needs, foster agility, and gain a competitive edge. You will be equipped with strategies for enhanced productivity, efficiency, adaptability, and value delivery.

Shifting from projects to products

In the evolution of digital knowledge work, there’s a pivotal shift from project-based to product-centric models, transforming how organizations deliver value and respond to market demands. Mik Kersten’s book Project to Product drew significant attention to this change, offering a structured framework and practical guidance that solidified the concept.1 Building upon discussions within the Agile and DevOps communities, Kersten’s work reinforced the importance of product-centric thinking, which Agile methodologies and the DevOps movement had already laid out.

Traditionally, the project-centric paradigm, often associated with the Waterfall development model, emphasized discrete, temporary efforts with a specific scope, budget, and timeline. However, this model proved to be inefficient, unnecessarily delaying projects and allowing coding errors and defects to accumulate, making them increasingly difficult to resolve. Moreover, it often led to siloed efforts focused solely on predetermined deliverables and meeting project milestones rather than on addressing end user needs or fostering continuous improvement.

The modern transition to a product-centric approach represents a fundamental shift in strategy and execution in the digital landscape. Unlike projects, products are seen as living entities that evolve with customer needs and market trends. This shift aligns with Lean, Agile, and DevOps practices, emphasizing long-term value creation, cross-functional collaboration, and a relentless focus on customer experience and feedback loops.

This transition facilitates a more adaptable and responsive organizational culture, where teams are empowered to iterate and innovate rapidly. The product-centric model fosters a sense of ownership and accountability, as teams are continuously engaged with their product throughout its life cycle, leading to higher quality outcomes and greater alignment with business objectives.

Incorporating the product-oriented approach requires a fundamental shift in mindsets, processes, and management structures within organizations. It aligns more closely with the needs of digital knowledge work, where agility, responsiveness, and continuous improvement are key to sustaining a competitive advantage in a rapidly evolving digital marketplace.

Evaluating project-centric behaviors

In project-centric environments, teams exhibit several distinct behaviors that shape their approach to work and collaboration:

  • Fixed scope emphasis: Teams prioritize delivering specific outputs within predefined constraints, focusing heavily on adherence to the original plan, budget, and timeline.
  • Temporary teams: People are allocated to projects and disbanded upon completion. From project-to-project, team composition frequently changes, leading to potential disruptions in knowledge continuity and team dynamics.
  • Change resistance: Change is often seen as a threat to the project’s scope, budgets, and timelines, leading to rigid change control processes that can slow adaptation.
  • Success metrics: Success is measured by the project’s adherence to predefined specifications, deadlines, and budget rather than long-term value or customer satisfaction.
  • Siloed workflows: Work tends to occur in departmental or functional silos, with limited cross- functional collaboration, often resulting in hand-offs that can delay delivery and reduce efficiency.

Now that we’ve established a common understanding of project-centered behaviors, let’s explore how these behaviors evolve as organizations transition toward product-centric development strategies.

Transitioning behaviors

As organizations transition toward product-centric development strategies, they exhibit transitional behaviors characterized by a shift toward balanced priorities, flexible team structures, openness to change, evolving success metrics, and integrated workflows. Let’s explore these behaviors in more detail.

  • Balanced priorities: Organizations begin to balance the need for delivering specific project outputs with understanding the broader impact on customer value and strategic goals.
  • Flexible team structures: There’s an increased emphasis on stable, cross-functional teams, though they may still be project-bound. Teams start to form around capabilities and products.
  • Openness to change: Changes are managed more flexibly, with a growing understanding that adaptation can lead to better outcomes. Iterative methodologies such as Agile may facilitate this.
  • Evolving success metrics: While traditional project metrics remain important, there’s an increasing focus on measures such as customer feedback and market impact.
  • Integrated workflows: Efforts to break down silos begin, with increased emphasis on collaboration across functions and stages of work.

As we progress through the transition from project-centric to product-centric behaviors, let’s now explore the transformative shift toward product-centric approaches. Here, teams prioritize continuous value delivery, embrace change, and foster seamless collaboration.

Contrasting product-centric behaviors

As organizations transition to product-centric behaviors, they prioritize continuous value delivery, embrace change, and foster seamless collaboration. For example, they focus on enabling flow demanded by a continuous, holistic product focus as described below.

  • Customer and market focus: Teams are primarily focused on delivering continuous value to customers and responding to market changes. The scope is flexible and evolves based on feedback and insights.
  • Persistent, cross-functional teams: Teams are stable, long-term, and organized around products or services. They possess all the necessary skills to deliver value from end-to-end.
  • Embrace change: Change is seen as an opportunity to learn and improve. Teams rapidly adapt based on real-world feedback, using method such as continuous delivery and deployment.
  • Value-based success metrics: Success is measured by the product’s impact on the market, customer satisfaction, and the ability to deliver business outcomes over the long term.
  • Seamless collaboration: Workflows are highly integrated, with a strong emphasis on transparency, communication, and collaboration across all levels and functions within the organization.

Product-based organizations are in a constant state of evolution, moving from rigid, project structures and behaviors towards more dynamic, product-oriented models that prioritize agility, customer value, and sustainable growth.

Let’s examine flow at different levels, macro and micro, to enable the greater emphasis on flow that continuous, holistic, product-focus demands.

Understanding flow at organizational and individual levels

In this subsection, we explore how identifying constraints within processes and employing principles such as the theory of constraints, Lean flow, and metrics can systematically address these challenges, leading to a more efficient and effective flow of work and value creation. Addressing these challenges helps us achieve more with fewer resources, maintain high-quality standards, deliver enhanced customer value, foster employee satisfaction, and accelerate deliveries.

As we explore the process of streamlining value streams, we must examine two distinct levels of flow: the macro level, encompassing the organization’s collective workflow, and the micro level, focusing on individual employees’ personal work experiences.

Let’s take a closer look at these levels to gain a comprehensive understanding of how optimizing flow can lead to improved efficiency and satisfaction:

  • Macro level (collective flow): This perspective includes the entire organization’s workflows, which ensures cohesion and efficiency. Like a relay race, these flows involve a synchronized, cross-organizational flow that allows work to progress rapidly and efficiently through the usual stages of planning, developing, testing, and deploying. This macro approach reflects how lean flow principles improve productivity and efficiency while leveraging Agile’s iterative and incremental planning process, fostering adaptability and evolution across our value stream flows. The macro level effectively represents a view of our value stream flows.
  • Micro level (personal flow): On an individual level, flow relates to an employee’s work experience. It involves uninterrupted time, deep and focused work without the risk of burnout, a dedication to one’s area of expertise, and the need for acknowledgment. The personal flow is illustrated by the balance between challenge and skill, which is fundamental to achieving joy and satisfaction at work, as depicted in Figure 6.3.

Both the macro and micro levels of flow are critically interdependent. Enhancing flow within an organization should include efforts to do the following:

  • Reduce delays
  • Lower friction
  • Increase engagement
  • Boost performance

When seeking improvement, people often tend to concentrate on either the macro or micro aspects. However, both aspects must be considered simultaneously to establish high-performance systems. When addressing the macro level, measure the impact on the individual and vice versa.

As we explore optimizing value streams further, our next step is to identify obstacles to flow. These challenges obstruct work, create confusion, and reduce productivity. By identifying the most frequent types of flow interruptions, we can understand why unintentional value streams form. Recognizing and eliminating these obstacles is necessary because they persist when the flow isn’t intentionally designed to avoid them.

Identifying obstacles to streamlined workflows

Making value flow without interruption is the first step to optimizing flow, as obstacles persist when workflows lack intentional design to address these problems. Figure 6.3 depicts typical challenges, highlighting how they impede the efficient delivery of products and services:

Figure 6.3 – The path to value often winds through a maze of challenges

The following is a list of typical impediments to flow:

  • Lack of clarity: This causes confusion and misdirection, leading to wasted effort and resources, as team members may not understand the goals or processes.
  • Unclear approvals: Unclear approvals cause delays and bottlenecks in a value stream, disrupting the smooth flow of processes.
  • Frequent handoffs: This disrupts the flow by increasing the chances of miscommunication, delays, and errors. They can lead to inefficiencies and cause longer overall process time.
  • Bottlenecks and queues: They slow progress as tasks get queued, waiting for previous activities to be completed, reducing overall efficiency and increasing lead times.
  • Dependencies: You may encounter a situation where one team’s work is blocked while another team’s related work is completed. These handoffs typically occur when there is a gap in responsibility, knowledge, collaboration, and fast feedback.
  • Excessive work in process (WIP): Too much WIP causes priorities and resources to be shuffled, tasks to be frequently switched, and operating expenses to increase. It reduces productivity and throughput and increases wait times for new functionality.
  • Rework: This is a waste of time and resources, as completed work needs to be done again due to errors or changes in requirements.
  • Technical debt: The cost of deferring fixes or addressing identified issues, which leads to increased maintenance costs and reduced agility over time as accumulated shortcuts or incomplete solutions must be corrected later.
  • Escalations and interruptions: These distract from planned work, often requiring immediate attention, disrupting workflow, and leading to project delays.

Seeing the organization in terms of flow

Eliminating the constraints imposed by traditional hierarchical structures improves productivity and efficiency, enabling a smoother flow of work across functional silos. Dr. Melvin Conway’s article in Datamation, titled ‘How Do Committees Invent?’, discusses the relationship between organizational and technical design. His conclusion was that “Organizations which design systems (in the broad sense used here) are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations.” 2 Simply put, the architecture of a system mirrors the communication structure of the organization that designed it.

What does Conway’s law mean in practice? “Suppose the technology department at ABC Insurance has three main workgroups: one focused on insurance claims, a second for underwriting, and a third for actuarial analysis. Conway’s law predicts that your system design will mirror your organizational structure and communication pathways. In turn, there will be a separate software module for each of these three capabilities. Each will likely have a unique architecture, design, infrastructure, and systems integration protocols. The implications of team structure on architecture are often overlooked. The team’s organizational design should align with the desired technical architecture, not the other way around.”3

In traditional hierarchical and domain-based structures, work tends to align with these siloed teams and departments. It obstructs visibility into how value flows across the organization, making it challenging to see bottlenecks and inefficiencies. Furthermore, the additional cross-departmental and cross-functional processes required to overcome these silos create excess processing waste, which means doing more work or adding more components than necessary to meet customer requirements. Creating pathways to value – understanding and identifying value streams

Within an organization lies a complex network of value streams, each serving as a vital channel for delivering value to both internal and external customers. Recognizing these streams is crucial, yet it can be a complex task for organizations unfamiliar with the concept or evolving toward a value-oriented framework.

To demystify these streams, begin with the customer outcomes that signify success for the organization. A customer outcome, which goes beyond the general idea of customer satisfaction, identifies the exact value provided, such as a feature’s utilization, new customer acquisition, or a completed sale.

Consider the sales process, an internal value stream that mirrors a customer’s journey in evaluating their business or user needs, reviewing competitive offerings, and making informed purchasing decisions. This process experiences variable lead times, influenced by factors such as customer product reviews, competitive value analysis, and buying processes. While inherently operational, the sales-oriented value stream remains adaptable and open to improvement through Agile methodologies applied to organizational CRM tools, sales processes, pre-sales roles, availability of competitive and customer information, and product capabilities.

In the previous subsection, we emphasized the significance of superimposing a value stream across our organizational structures to better appreciate how work flows. But how do we define the value stream in the first place? Discovering a value stream involves a reverse journey from the customer outcome, inquiring about the processes and decisions that lead to the realization of that outcome. This backward journey often leads to the source of the process, which could be a customer request, a stakeholder’s need, or an output from another value stream.

Initiating our value stream design by first defining what our customers want ensures that we stay aligned with their needs throughout the process. As we map the journey backward customer delivery upstream across our value stream flow to the initial order, we continuously inquire about the information and other product or service components that must be delivered at each stage of our value stream. This iterative approach guides us step by step, eventually leading us all the way back to the point of the initial customer order or request. It serves as a customer-centric foundation upon which we trigger our value stream flow.

In the process of optimizing our software delivery pipeline, we work our way backward through each phase of the software development life cycle (SDLC). Our goal is to ensure that each stage is seamlessly integrated and aligned with the principles of customer-centric value stream design. To achieve this, we incorporate the essential elements of value streams: people, process, information, and technology. As we navigate our way upstream from support all the way back to concept or the initial order/request—we will define the critical components that contribute to a robust and efficient software development process.

This backward exploration of value stream flows is not strictly applied to software delivery pipelines. All value steams need to be assessed in this manner to make informed improvements. Ideally, software delivery teams participate in these assessments to evaluate areas where software enhancements can improve the targeted value stream.

Once identified, value streams become concrete elements that an organization can optimize to improve value delivery. They are actionable avenues where businesses can exercise control to transform customer interactions into reliable, gratifying experiences that propel organizational success.

By mapping these streams, an organization not only gains insight into its operational status quo but also lays the groundwork for targeted enhancements that streamline the flow of value. Embracing this method promotes ongoing improvement to enhance each stream and amplify customer value.

This is the summit where value is perceived and realized by the customer. To reach this peak, one must consider the product or service being offered and the journey the customer undertakes to interact with this offering.

Refining the process – questions for progress

Another critical point to consider is that Figures 6.11 to 6.13 underscore a fundamental principle: the coordinates at which a stream is placed within this bi-dimensional framework directly result from what it does, prioritizes, and measures. However, these figures do not show how a stream might move after considering the results of priority and measurement. To shift a stream, the team adjusts its priorities and measurements. As a result, we can see not only the present flow of activities but also a future state flow by defining changes to targets that drive changes in behavior.

Paraphrasing the teachings of Eliyahu M. Goldratt, the author of The Goal and the developer of the theory of constraints, it can be said that “the elements an organization chooses to measure will inevitably align with its achievements.” He is also often quoted as saying, “Show me how you’ll measure me, and I’ll show you how I’ll behave.” Said another way, your outcomes are a downstream effect of what you measure upstream. This is because the act of measurement does more than just record data—it focuses attention and resources, dictating what the organization will actually execute.

So, with this information, we can answer these questions on the value of metrics and measurements:

  • Why do metrics and measurements matter?

    Understanding the influence of metrics and measurement on organizational positioning is not just an academic exercise; it is crucial to tangible progress in any business domain. It matters because the metrics we prioritize are the rudders steering our collective efforts. They shape our decision-making processes, influence our resource allocation, and ultimately define our success criteria.

  • How do we release more often?

    To enhance the frequency of our releases, we should focus on the efficiency of our release mechanisms. By measuring the lead time and the proportion of automated processes versus manual interventions, we can identify bottlenecks. Tracking the influence of constraints and the handoffs between teams can reveal inefficiencies and areas ripe for innovation.

  • How can we change the way we work?

    Effective alteration of our work methods requires a shift in what we measure. By setting new performance indicators, we influence behaviors and reshape the operational model. The focus of the team naturally shifts to align with these new metrics, driving them to adapt their approaches and innovate their practices.

  • What if we want to change [variable]?

    If a change is desired in a specific area, noted as “[variable]” in the question, it is essential to start by identifying the relevant metrics that can trigger a change in that domain. The E9 VSM Methodology presented in Chapter 4 offers a robust approach to conducting a VSM engagement. But for the purposes of this chapter, we can simplify it. The objectives for each of the five key steps are:

Streamlining value delivery

Navigating through the intricacies of value stream management requires a focus on several pivotal elements that ensure a seamless and dynamic flow of value to the customer:

  • Delivery focus: The core emphasis lies in the sustainable delivery of value. This involves a persistent commitment to not just planning or outlining the value streams but operationalizing and executing them efficiently.
  • Topology supporting value streams: A well-defined team topology is the foundation of successful value streams. The interaction of multiple teams within a network facilitates a comprehensive, interconnected system that propels value delivery forward.
  • Set new targets: Shifting the performance focus necessitates setting new targets and benchmarks. By altering what is measured and expected, behaviors and outcomes within the value stream are redirected toward the desired change.
  • Visualize flow across the organization: It is crucial to understand and see the flow of value not just in isolated pockets but across the entire organizational structure. This broad perspective is essential for a holistic approach to value stream management, ensuring that all parts of the organization are aligned and contributing to the flow of value.

Empowering transformation: orchestrating change with platform teams

In this transformative journey from traditional IT functions to streamlined value streams, one of the key enablers of change has been the emergence of platform teams. These specialized teams represent a paradigm shift in how organizations approach software development. They act as architects of efficiency, weaving together the tools, services, and capabilities that empower development teams to navigate the turbulent seas of dependencies and inefficiencies.

Platform teams, in the context described, refer to specialized groups or teams within an organization that focus on developing and maintaining shared platforms, tools, and capabilities that enable other development teams to work more efficiently and effectively. These platform teams are responsible for creating a set of services and resources that streamline and simplify the development process for other value-stream teams across the organization.

Platform Teams:

Specialized groups within a software delivery organization that develop and maintain the underlying infrastructure, tools, and services that support and enable the efficient development, deployment, and operation of applications.

Key characteristics of platform teams include the following:

  • Self-service: Platform teams offer self-service access to their tools and resources, allowing other teams to use them without extensive manual intervention or support
  • Tools and capabilities: They provide a range of tools, libraries, and infrastructure components that help development teams automate tasks, adhere to best practices, and accelerate their development cycles
  • Responsiveness: Platform teams are responsive to the needs of their internal customers (other development teams) and work closely with them to understand their requirements and pain points
  • Standardization: They establish and maintain standardized practices and patterns across the organization, ensuring consistency and reducing complexity
  • Scalability: Platform teams design their service delivery solutions to be scalable, accommodating the needs of various development teams and projects
  • Continuous improvement: They continuously evolve and improve their offerings based on feedback and changing technology landscapes

In essence, platform teams act as enablers, providing the infrastructure and tools necessary for other teams to focus on their core objectives, such as developing software products, while minimizing common operational challenges and dependencies. This approach promotes agility, accelerates development, and enhances overall efficiency within the organization.
