Connecting the Value Stream Network

“Success today is not about surviving change; it’s about leading it.” – Peter Drucker

Imagine your organization as a vast communications network, coordinating work, information, and resources from initial ideas to final outcomes, from requests to deliveries. As business and IT professionals, your success hinges on managing these complex flows efficiently to support customer journeys and drive business outcomes. How do you ensure that these processes run smoothly across your organization?

Managing these disparate pieces of work requires more than a single value stream—it demands coordinated efforts across streams aligned with customer needs.

Value Stream Networks (VSNs) are interconnected pathways within your organization that are strategically aligned to deliver holistic value. VSNs show how work items flow through multiple value streams while supporting a broader network that serves our business, stakeholders, and customer needs.

The seamless flow of work and information across this network maximizes the value delivered through products and services. However, these interconnections can introduce complexities, constraints, and potential waste that must be managed effectively. The network’s ability to create value depends on maintaining reliable, scalable, and efficient operations.

Even if individual value streams are streamlined, their interconnected nature and constant evolution introduce complexities. This chapter presents strategies to manage these complexities.

A VSN can function to some degree without visualization and measurement. However, without a visual representation of the value stream network, the organization will struggle to improve workflows and risk local optimizations that may have detrimental side effects.

This chapter covers the following topics:

  • Managing and optimizing value stream networks
  • Visualizing organizational workflows
  • Guiding value stream strategies and investments
  • Evolving the value stream network

Managing and optimizing value stream networks

Value stream networks, as defined by Mik Kersten in his book From Project to Products, refer to a holistic approach that captures and analyzes the movement of work within an organization1. Value stream networks provide a holistic view of an organization by illustrating how people, processes, information, and technology integrate to optimize customer value delivery. This approach helps shift mindsets from project-based thinking to a product-centric and customer-focused perspective.

VSNs foster collaboration among teams and processes, ensuring efficient value delivery across the organization. At their core, they facilitate the progression of value from inception to delivery, encompassing interactions, handoffs, and dependencies among various teams. For instance, when you order a product online, multiple teams—from design and development to fulfillment and marketing—collaborate to ensure its successful delivery.

Dynamic and responsive, VSNs evolve continually in response to software updates, market shifts, and budget adjustments, requiring ongoing adaptation for sustained effectiveness. Drawing on principles akin to graph theory, which studies the connections (edges) between points (nodes) in a network, they navigate diverse pathways across teams, offering insights beyond traditional hierarchical organizational charts.

Understanding VSNs is essential for grasping the intricate network that drives organizational efficiency and value delivery. It transcends organizational charts, offering a deeper understanding of how ideas translate into tangible outcomes.

Integrating multiple value streams within VSNs serves several key objectives:

  • End-to-end visibility and coordination: Organizations gain a holistic view of how work and information flow across different departments, functions, and processes, enhancing coordination toward common business objectives
  • Optimization of value delivery: By identifying and eliminating inefficiencies, organizations optimize the speed and efficiency of value delivery to customers
  • Cross-functional collaboration: VSNs promote collaboration among diverse teams, breaking down silos and improving problem-solving capabilities and innovation
  • Continuous improvement: Supporting a culture of continuous improvement, organizations analyze processes, gather insights, and implement changes to enhance performance and adapt to market demands
  • Customer-centricity: Integrating value streams helps organizations align activities with customer needs, enhancing satisfaction and loyalty

While often associated with Lean-Agile methodologies in software development, VSN principles apply broadly across various industries that employ Lean’s value stream concepts:

  • Manufacturing: Optimizing material flows and production processes
  • Healthcare: Streamlining patient care and payer processes for better outcomes
  • Supply chain management: Enhancing inventory management and responsiveness
  • Service industries: Improving customer service processes and satisfaction
  • Government and non-profit sectors: Enhancing service delivery and efficient resource allocation

In essence, value stream networks drive operational efficiency, promote collaboration, foster continuous improvement, and enhance customer-centricity across diverse sectors beyond software development.

Organizing value stream networks

In this subsection, we will look at how value stream networks are applied within an organization. Instead of visualizing our network as a shapeless web, we’ll anchor it to one or more customer journeys, highlighting its proximity to the customer and depicting the focus of its activities to either development or operations. Additionally, we can simulate and hypothesize the effects of changes in measurement, operating model, and strategy for each stream, as well as the network.

Adopting a value stream network model for software delivery includes all the tasks that are necessary to produce software, as well as the integration of people, processes, information, and technology as enabling capabilities that support the software development lifecycle and across all value stream flows. This model covers the entire end-to-end process, from the initial idea or customer request through development, testing, deployment, and ongoing support.

But value stream networks are not just about connecting software development activities. As you navigate this chapter, you will discover the importance of connecting all value streams to support our customers' journeys. In turn, we align our software delivery streams to assist in the improvement of all operational, development, and support value streams. Value stream management provides the methods and tools we use to identify and prioritize our improvement activities.

Defining customer journeys

Customer journeys have become a popular method to consider value delivery from the customer’s perspective and an invaluable reference for product management in recent decades. Customer journeys can be mapped, measured, and used to drive customer and outcome-centric conversations. They serve as an excellent reference point for technology leaders to engage business leaders on common ground. It’s also a far more valuable context than mapping the network with a strictly internal or isolated scope.

The business exists to create value for customers, while the customer journey provides a common foundation for teams and organizations as they begin to embrace the concept of value streams. It offers an outside-in, customer-centric perspective and a visual tool for understanding value delivery from the customer (or business outcomes) backward to an idea or request.

It’s crucial to recognize the distinction between customer journeys and a value stream. The customer journey, as depicted in Figure 7.2, is mostly beyond the organization’s influence. It takes place at the borders of the organization, where customers interact with products and services. Its path is primarily determined by customers, which may limit the organization’s ability to fully influence or optimize it.

Figure 7.2 – An illustration of a typical customer journey

For example, consider two customers: one is referred to a product by a friend and buys it immediately. The other discovers our company’s products or services through advertising, investigates alternatives, gets distracted by other needs, and only returns to purchase when timing, needs, and cost align. If these two scenarios were viewed through the lens of value streams, they would have dramatically different paths and attributes. Lead times for these scenarios would be significantly different. Furthermore, there are insufficient mechanisms for measuring work in process, value-added time, or percent complete and accurate for customer interactions. Customer journeys are typically measured by sentiment, probability of purchase, cost of acquisition, conversion rate, order size, and more.

In contrast, organizations have significant control over how their value stream operates. While they cannot dictate customer actions, they can implement standardization and automation to facilitate improved flow. Key metrics such as lead time, value-added time, throughput, quality, and work in process are essential. Organizations can guide work through a value stream much more effectively than they can direct a customer’s journey.

The customer journey serves as a foundation for understanding value streams because without it, value streams have no clear direction or focus. They also have no clear connection to other streams in the network. With the customer journey anchoring flow, we can connect value streams to demonstrate how they motivate and support the customer journey at critical touchpoints.

For instance, a marketing campaign value stream aids in a customer’s discovery and knowledge, potentially leading to a sale, while the sales and purchase processes—streamlined through easy-to-use systems such as checkouts or shopping carts—represent separate value streams that culminate in the purchase. This means that when we experience an issue with the customer journey, we can trace it directly to the contributing value streams. Connecting value streams to customer journeys

The journey of a given customer is an individualized path that often eludes the direct control of an organization. It starts with an innate need or want, sparking curiosity that leads to the discovery of a product or service. As customers move through various stages such as sale, purchase, use, and review, they follow a course that is inherently personal and self-directed. The customer journey can be envisioned as a series of interconnected experiences, each potentially influenced by different value streams within the organization.

Figure 7.5 depicts an enterprise Software as a Service (SaaS) customer journey. It starts with campaign exposure to make customers aware of a product via marketing efforts, so that they then try the product in a limited capacity, choose to purchase it, and progress through the customer experience. The figure demonstrates how each stage of the customer journey is supported by value streams consisting of planning, designing, building, validating, and releasing activities to ensure a seamless and engaging customer experience.

Figure 7.5 – Connecting value streams to the customer journey

The visualization of this journey lays the foundation for understanding how value streams operate and directly impact customer outcomes in a Lean-Agile organization. By orchestrating work across value streams, we create significant opportunities for refinement and optimization. Through standardization and automation, organizations can shape these value streams to effectively support the customer journey.

At the outset, a marketing campaign value stream plays a pivotal role in triggering the customer’s journey from initial curiosity to product use, product sales, and support. By allowing the customer to trial a product or service, it sets the stage for a potential sale. The journey then transitions into more tactical value streams, such as the sales process, where streamlined systems such as intuitive checkouts or shopping carts facilitate the purchase phase.

The product development value stream becomes especially crucial as it aligns with the use phase of the customer journey. It is here that the quality and features of the product come to the forefront, influencing the customer’s direct experience and satisfaction. Subsequent stages, such as review and recommendation, may be enhanced by other supportive value streams focused on customer engagement and loyalty.

Ultimately, while the primary value stream of product development enables the customer to complete their journey, the importance of the supporting value streams cannot be understated. They are the threads that weave through the customer’s experiences, ensuring that each transition is seamless and each interaction is meaningful. Together, these value streams form a spectrum that not only defines but also enriches the customer’s journey.

Addressing organizational challenges

Most organizations face the daunting task of improving efficiency and quality while managing a growing backlog of identified improvement opportunities. These challenges are interconnected and tackling them requires a strategic approach that hinges on optimizing operational flow.

In the following list, we will explore the work involved:

  • Achieving more with available resources: Businesses need to achieve better outcomes with the resources they have as they scale and compete. This means streamlining processes, eliminating waste, and leveraging technology to enhance their ability to deliver the most value. Mapping the value stream network starting with customer outcomes, we can re-engineer flow to emphasize what is most critical to value creation and minimize non-value-added activity, problematic dependencies, and waste.
  • Elevating quality standards: Quality cannot be compromised in the quest for efficiency. Organizations must find ways to enhance their quality control processes, such as building quality to ensure that the products or services delivered meet or exceed customer expectations. By mapping dependencies and the full flow of activities to outcomes across the organization, we can gain insight into which efforts will have the greatest impact on delivered quality. We can zoom in on details and zoom out to see interconnected impacts.
  • Enhancing value delivery: The goal here is to provide more value to customers, which could be achieved through innovative solutions, better service, or more responsive support. Companies must continuously evolve to meet and anticipate customer needs. By incorporating the customer journey in our network map, we can gain the ability to more closely attribute efforts and investments to identify the greatest impacts on customer value, whether those be upstream or downstream, and at what proximity to the customer they are.
  • Fostering talent satisfaction: Keeping employees happy is essential for maintaining a creative, innovative, and efficient workforce. This involves creating a work environment that supports and encourages employee growth and satisfaction. By providing a holistic representation of value creation and delivery across the organization, we can ensure that contributors can see how their work adds value, connect their efforts to business objectives, and understand their work in the context of customer outcomes.
  • Accelerating throughput: The need for speed is ever-present. Organizations must refine their workflows to deliver products and services faster without sacrificing quality. Visualizing interdependent flows of work enables understanding why certain streams are under- or over-utilized, as well as where the constraints causing bottlenecks are. We can identify critical and centralized capabilities and ensure that they operate at optimal performance in the long term.

Implementing a methodical approach to tackling flow challenges is crucial. By identifying the constraints within processes and employing principles such as the theory of constraints, Lean flow, and Lean metrics, businesses can systematically address these issues to create a more efficient and effective flow of work that drives value creation and delivery. Putting this interconnected work in the context of value, customers, and business outcomes facilitates a greater focus on what matters to the organization and its long-term success.

Value stream network (top level)

At the top level, we can see the big picture of the value stream network, which encapsulates the macro perspective of how work is delivered within the organization. This is the broadest view, where the focus is on how value streams connect holistically from start to finish without delving into the details of individual components or technical dependencies.

Team topologies (middle level)

Moving to the middle level of the visualization, we encounter team topologies, a model that outlines how teams within the organization are structured and how they interact. This level zooms in from the end-to-end flow to show the topology of teams, such as stream-aligned teams working alongside complicated-subsystem teams, and supported by platform teams that provide foundational services.

Team topologies, as defined by Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais in their 2019 book Team Topologies: Organizing Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow, are concepts in organizational design and software delivery.2 These topologies describe how teams are structured and interact within an organization to optimize workflow, reduce bottlenecks, and enhance collaboration. Skelton and Pais provide valuable insights into how various team types, such as stream-aligned teams, complicated-subsystem teams, and platform teams, can be organized to support an organization’s goals and facilitate efficient value delivery.

Beyond mere structure, they define three interaction modes —Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, and facilitation—to make the connections between teams explicitly defined operating models.3 In the context of VSM, an operating model can be thought of as a blueprint that outlines how an organization delivers value to its customers, manages its resources, and achieves its strategic goals.

By establishing clarity on how teams interact, we can avoid many of the pitfalls of siloed operations where handoffs (the baton) of the value stream “relay race” are dropped, delayed, or fumbled.

Value chain capabilities (bottom level)

At the bottom (or micro) level, the graphic delves into the specifics of the capabilities and components that compose and contribute to delivered value. This level of detail is where individual features and functions are identified and examined for their contribution to the customer journey, from the initial concept to the final delivery and support.

Guiding value stream strategy and investments

The Wardley map in Figure 7.8 provides a visual tool that helps organizations understand their business environment and strategize accordingly. It helps us answer the question “Where should we invest?” and, to a lesser extent, “How can we deliver?” 4.

This map consists of two main components:

  • Value chain (y-axis): This shows the components needed to meet user needs, from visible user-facing components to hidden infrastructure. A component is any element or part of a system (or value chain) that contributes to delivering a product or service to the end user.
  • Evolution (x-axis): This indicates the stages of evolution of each component, ranging from genesis (novel and uncertain) to commodity (ubiquitous and well-defined). A value chain is the series of activities that comprise the life cycle of a product or service, from genesis through production to purchase by the end customer.

In our use case, we are evaluating whether we should invest in an AI-based imaging tool to improve our value stream.

Figure 7.8 – Wardley helps answer where to invest and how to deliver

This map will help our organization answer strategic questions, such as the following:

  • Where should we invest in innovation?
  • How can we anticipate changes in our competitive landscape?

By visualizing these elements and their relationships, organizations can make more informed decisions about resource allocation, innovation, and competitive positioning. It’s a valuable tool for strategic planning, particularly in technology and business contexts.

By mapping the interconnected capabilities of an organization with customer needs, this framework provides a comprehensive overview that balances the importance of innovation with the inevitability of commoditization.

Evolving the value stream network

As organizations evolve, they increasingly seek a comprehensive understanding of their operational dynamics and how to influence them. Combining the axes of Customer Proximity and Performance Focus allows leaders to pinpoint each value stream’s position relative to its visibility to customers and its performance, as illustrated in Figure 7.10.

For example, value streams such as mobile banking are visible to customers and require metrics that are focused on customer satisfaction such as net promoter score (NPS) and customer engagement. These are the front-facing elements of the business where innovation and responsiveness to customer needs are paramount.

Figure 7.10 – Balancing efficiency and innovation performance focus

Conversely, back-office processes such as billing are invisible to customers and reside in the efficiency range of performance focus. These activities are measured against standards of operational excellence and the metrics include cost, standardization, predictability, and consistency. The strategic considerations for these processes might include the potential for outsourcing to external providers, streamlining operations, or automating to reduce costs.

This dynamic model provides insights into how changes in one area—such as the introduction of FinOps or the automation of platforms—can ripple through the network, affecting adjacent streams. The integration of new capabilities into a value stream such as mobile banking raises questions about adaptation. For example, how should mobile banking adapt to the introduction of automated FinOps platforms? What are the potential impacts on customer experience, and what new efficiencies or innovations might emerge?

The implications for behavior and strategy are profound. Each connection, each node on this map, represents a decision point—a place where the balance between innovation and efficiency, between customer-facing actions and back-end processes, must be carefully managed to maintain an effective value stream network. Understanding these connections and the alignment of internal processes with overarching organizational goals is crucial for strategic agility.

The value stream network is not static; it embodies the potential for movement, reflecting an organization’s strategic flexibility. A compelling example of this dynamism is the evolution of Amazon Web Services (AWS). What began as an internal infrastructure program to support Amazon’s operations transformed AWS from an internal capability to an external value stream. This change underscores the significance of measuring and valuing various aspects of the value stream over time, aligning with opportunities and realizing new value.

Organizations must understand where their value streams are positioned and how they can be improved over time. There are many benefits of such strategic movements, from delivering value to customers and optimizing internal processes to maintaining a competitive edge. By understanding and managing this evolutionary path, organizations can unlock new opportunities and create value in ways that were not possible before.

This type of evolution has an impact on our value stream networks, as they, too, must transition. We’ll explore this concept in the next subsection.

Improving value streams by performance focus

The decision to improve with sustaining innovation may lead to a reorganization of existing value streams, which should be a deliberate and thoughtful process of evolution rather than a radical disruption. This approach can reduce waste and political risks, as well as leverage existing expertise and capabilities.

Figure 7.11 illustrates the adaptation of a value stream by performance focus, highlighting the shift from innovation to an efficiency focus.

Figure 7.11 – Adapting value streams by performance focus

For many organizations, disruptive innovation—starting afresh with no legacy constraints— is strong. However, this approach often requires significant investment and comes with its own set of risks. Alternatively, by incrementally shifting how an existing process such as billing operates, organizations can evolve their value streams to achieve different, often more innovative outcomes without discarding the underlying systems and processes that have served them well.

This shift involves a conscious decision to move away from established measures and behaviors toward new goals. For example, a billing system traditionally focused on efficiency might begin to incorporate more innovative approaches such as adopting new technologies or modifying workflows to improve the customer experience, automation, or performance. These changes can lead to a more dynamic and responsive billing process that still retains the core advantages of the existing system, such as reliability and predictability.

This transition is about adapting to new environments and challenges while retaining what has proven successful. To be clear, this is the evolution of the operating model, not technology or capability. Leaders, therefore, need to focus on understanding and defining the new metrics that will measure success. By redefining these metrics, the organization encourages behaviors that drive innovation within the context of established value streams.

In summary, by intentionally shifting existing value streams, organizations can find a middle ground that achieves the best of the sustaining and disruptive innovation approaches. In turn, this allows for the evolution of services and processes in a way that is both innovative and grounded in the proven foundations of the past.

Using this map and digging deeper into flow bottlenecks for activities, we can begin to see the network beyond the value stream and the capabilities that lie even deeper within.

Value stream mapping acts as a lens through which the interconnected nature of cross-departmental workflows becomes apparent and the network emerges. These interdependencies often reflect areas for optimization. For example, manual processes could be automated into a more streamlined self-service workflow, boosting the value stream’s efficiency.

However, not every workflow identified through value stream mapping may currently represent a mature value stream. The map is likely to include potential value streams at various stages of maturity and automation. Recognizing these stages allows an organization to prioritize development efforts strategically, focusing on the areas with the most potential for refinement and automation to bolster efficiency and value.

Value stream mapping goes beyond identifying the current workflow. It serves as a diagnostic, enabling us to dissect and comprehend the fabric of our organizational processes. These mapping efforts guide where we can achieve the most impact, turning problems and opportunities into measurable results. So, now that we know that we can use value stream mapping to expose our constraints, let’s explore how we can eliminate them to streamline our value flow.

It should be clear to you by now that this book consistently focuses on delivering the most value to customers. In the next section, we’ll explore how to build a customer-centric culture within our value streams.

Cultivating a customer-centric approach

Stream-aligned teams function as internal customers of the shared platform offerings, while platform teams provide self-service capabilities to enhance operational flow and product development. Therefore, platform teams need to adopt a customer service mindset, elevating the quality of service and fostering a culture of excellence and continuous improvement.

The relationship between stream-aligned teams and platform teams is key to our shared-services model. Stream-aligned teams, acting as internal customers, use platform capabilities to improve their operations and product development.

In many organizations, there’s a tendency to mandate the use of specific internal software development tools and platforms. However, to avoid counterproductive effects, executive management and platform teams must commit to enabling stream-aligned teams by providing competitive service and capabilities compared to external alternatives.

Rather than treating platform adoption as an obligation, it is essential to approach it with a customer service mindset. Platform teams should deliver their capabilities as if they were serving external customers, ensuring a level of service that rivals or exceeds external alternatives. Alternatively, the organization may consider outsourcing this service to a specialized platform support company. In any case, where there is a customer, there is a customer journey, and care must be taken to support it.

By treating internal deliverables with the same care and attention as products meant for the external market, platform teams can elevate the organization’s quality of service. This motivates stream-aligned teams to engage with the platform’s offerings, contributing to the overall health of the value stream network and establishing a culture of excellence and continuous improvement.
