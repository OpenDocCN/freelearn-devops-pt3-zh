Simplifying complexity – navigating Lean-Agile implementation challenges

A common theme throughout this book is that the journey toward achieving organizational agility and operational excellence can be realized through the adoption of Lean-Agile principles and methods. However, this path is often fraught with complexity, especially when applying the new paradigm within enterprise environments where people are resistant to change.

The art of simplifying complexity lies at the heart of effective Lean-Agile implementation. By eliminating organizational silos, we enhance work and information flows via value streams. We get increasingly better over time by identifying and eliminating activities that do not add value to our products or services. That’s the Lean part of a Lean-Agile Enterprise.

But that’s only half the story. Organizations must also be willing and able to continuously adapt and evolve to address changes in business requirements, market dynamics, and customer needs. By breaking up the complexity of change into smaller increments and iteratively finding new ways to improve, we get better with each new incremental delivery. That is the essence of Agile-based practices.

Let’s quickly explore what Lean-Agile practices entail at their most fundamental levels.

Applying Lean-Agile practices – streamlining processes and enhancing agility

The fundamental principles of Lean-Agile practices are straightforward. First, organize people, processes, technology, and information to facilitate seamless product delivery flows, eliminating organizational silos common in hierarchical structures. Second, identify and eliminate waste in activities that don’t add value from the customer’s perspective. This reduces costs, improves quality, and increases throughput. Eliminating constraints is equally crucial; an organization can only move as fast as its slowest activity. Trying to force more work into the system increases costs, bottlenecks, delays, and hidden defects. Finally, small, cross-functional teams should be implemented to tackle business problems, planning, and developing solutions iteratively and incrementally. For example, employ small cross-functional teams to resolve your constraints and other wastes. By following these practices, organizations can become both lean and agile.

While Lean-Agile practices are relatively simple to understand and learn, the complexity lies in changing the culture and mindset of the organization without tearing it apart in the process. So, next, we’ll look at customizing Lean-Agile practices to facilitate organizational transformation.

Customizing Lean-Agile practices for organizational transformation

A primary concern in Lean-Agile transformations is the potential mismatch between the proposed methods and the organization’s existing hierarchical or flattened structures and legacy practices. Hierarchies encourage siloed business activities, while flattened organizations often lack the structure to coordinate work and information flows. Neither of these extremes is ideal.

Without a thoughtful, context-sensitive approach, companies might be entangled in expensive and complex implementations that do more to disrupt than improve. This reality underscores the importance of a customized adaptation of Lean-Agile practices (or frameworks) tailored to the organization’s unique DNA.

Regardless of the Lean-Agile framework you choose, there is no out-of-the-box solution for your organization. Tailoring the use of methods and tools is always critical. You may find it more beneficial to mix and match practices to meet your needs situationally. While vendors will happily promote their knowledge bases, practices, and training, it may be more than you need and still not directly fit your needs. Meanwhile, your executive management team is responsible for finding cost-effective solutions for implementation, training, mentoring, and coaching. This requires full engagement from the executive sponsors.

When navigating the challenges of Lean-Agile implementation, it is crucial to acknowledge and respect the diversity of existing organizational landscapes. Each enterprise has its history, culture, and established processes, which must be considered when designing the transformation strategy.

Executives must deeply understand their operations to avoid common pitfalls such as misaligned roles and outdated enterprise-wide processes. This book has stressed the importance of GEMBA Walks, which involve going to where the work is being done to see firsthand how things work and what the issues are. Talking to the people doing the work is essential, as they best understand the problems and opportunities.

The insights gained serve as a compass for refining the implementation of Lean-Agile practices, ensuring they meet the organization’s needs for change while driving maximum efficiency and value delivery. Consequently, simplifying complexity isn’t about applying Lean-Agile principles by the book; it’s about interpreting them in the language of the organization. It’s a strategic exercise in customizing frameworks, tools, and practices to fit within the organization while propelling it forward.

Lean-Agile thus becomes a strategic asset underpinning the organization’s continual quest for excellence and growth. It’s considered “strategic” because Lean-Agile mastery contributes significantly to achieving strategic goals and objectives and is often central to the company’s value proposition.

Lean and Agile – two sides of the coin

Lean and Agile are complementary but distinct areas of focus for your operating model and should be balanced against each other to ensure smooth progress and performance. We discussed the developmental and operational spectrum of value streams in Chapter 6, with a deeper understanding of value streams as interconnected networks of people, processes, information, and technology in Chapter 7.

With its focus on adaptation and rapid delivery, agile techniques sit on the developmental side, striving for increased effectiveness with the associated uncertainty and inconsistency. Conversely, lean techniques emphasize the minimization of waste and the implementation of pull production control strategies and standardization where possible. Each balances the other to enable sustainable value delivery. The challenge is to leverage value stream concepts intelligently, applying Lean principles where appropriate to improve productivity and quality, and using Agile’s iterative and incremental development methods to drive change.

As a leader, your challenge is to ensure value streams are defined and the work is aligned with the tasks at hand. For day-to-day operational work, the goal is to create efficient and sustainable business practices that meet customer demands with high quality. However, other value streams must use Agile’s IID practices to create new solutions for improving business processes, systems, products, and services. Additionally, you need to ensure that your subject matter experts (SMEs) have time to support the development teams with their expertise.

Modern digital technologies integrate, automate, and synchronize work across traditional development boundaries, tremendously accelerating deliveries. For example, IT’s CI/CD and DevOps pipelines have reduced software development cycle times from months to days, or even less. In these situations, Agile practices set the stage for planning and analysis, while the actual development activities operate as Lean-oriented flows.

Fostering a culture of continuous learning and improvement

Leaders should create an organizational culture that values continuous learning and improvement. This involves encouraging experimentation, tolerating failures (but failing small) as learning opportunities, and regularly providing training and development resources. Such a culture supports Lean-Agile principles and practices by empowering employees to seek out inefficiencies and propose innovative solutions.

In the current business climate, the only constant is change. For organizations to thrive and stay competitive, they must embrace a culture of continuous learning and improvement. This culture is not a natural byproduct of daily operations; it must be cultivated and nurtured. Leaders play a pivotal role in weaving the new culture into the company’s fabric. Here are some ways leaders can achieve this:

  • Implement Lean-Agile principles: Adopt Lean-Agile methodologies emphasizing customer value, efficiency, and employee empowerment. These principles naturally encourage continuous improvement and learning.
  • Leading by example: Leadership starts at the top. Leaders must not only endorse continuous learning but also actively participate in it. By engaging in learning activities themselves, leaders set a precedent for all employees to follow.
  • Embedding learning in strategy: Make continuous learning integral to the company’s strategic objectives. This requires clear communication on the value of learning and its direct impact on achieving business goals.
  • Establish psychological safety: Encourage employees to experiment by creating a safe space where genuine efforts and sincere failures are not frowned upon but seen as a stepping stone to innovation. Reward efforts and learning, not just successful outcomes.
  • Failing small and early: Recognize that one of the key benefits of Agile’s iterative and incremental development approach is risk reduction, enabling teams to fail small, fast, and early, and using the failures for course corrections and learning experiences.
  • Embrace experimentation: Instead of penalizing failures, dissect them to understand their root cause. This will transform them into powerful lessons for the organization and prevent future repetition of the same missteps.
  • Provide training and development resources: Invest in diverse training programs that cater to different learning styles and professional paths. Ensure employees have the time and resources to engage with these learning opportunities.
  • Encourage knowledge sharing: Promote an environment where knowledge sharing is a norm. Tools such as internal messaging, wikis, regular “lunch and learn” sessions, and collaborative projects can facilitate this exchange.
  • Empower employees: Give employees the autonomy to identify inefficiencies and propose solutions. When individuals feel their input is valued, they are more likely to contribute proactively.
  • Frequent feedback cycles: Integrate regular feedback mechanisms to allow employees to understand how they are performing and where they can improve.
  • Recognize and reward: Publicly acknowledge individuals and teams who exemplify the spirit of continuous learning and improvement. Rewards can be intrinsic, such as recognition, or extrinsic, such as bonuses.
  • Use technology to enhance learning: Leverage commercial and in-house training platforms to provide easy access to educational resources.
  • Continuously reflect and adapt: Encourage regular reflection on both personal and organizational practices and be willing to adapt based on what is learned.

By embedding these principles into the organizational DNA, leaders will create a skilled and adaptable workforce and foster an environment where continuous improvement is the norm. This environment supports Lean-Agile principles for a responsive and resilient organization. It leads to a virtuous cycle where learning leads to improvement, and improvement opens new avenues for learning, ensuring that the organization survives and thrives in an unpredictable world.

Building cross-functional teams and encouraging collaboration

The siloed nature of traditional organizational structures can hinder the effective implementation of complex business processes. Leaders can drive change by fostering cross-functional teams that bring diverse skills and perspectives together within a value stream to deliver specific components of an integrated increment. Recall from Chapter 8 (BLAST) and Chapter 9 (BASE) that integrated increments take the form of minimum viable products (MVPs), minimum valuable increments (MVIs), and minimum valuable releases (MVRs). Encouraging collaboration through BLAST teams and teams of teams enhances problem-solving and innovation, making the organization more adaptable to change.

Traditional organizational structures often compartmentalize expertise within distinct departments, leading to what is commonly referred to as silos. These silos can create barriers to communication and workflow, impeding the organization’s ability to implement complex business processes efficiently and effectively. Worse, departments can become bloated in large part due to the inefficiencies of managing cross-departmental workflows and disconnect from the primary purpose of the organization – which is to deliver value to their customers through their products and services.

To combat this, leaders can champion the formation of cross-functional teams, which are collaborative groups of people with varied expertise and perspectives from across the organization. Building cross-functional teams is a strategic initiative that promotes a holistic approach to tackling business problems. Drawing together diverse skill sets, these teams are better equipped to view problems from multiple angles, leading to more robust solutions. This diversity of thought is particularly valuable in problem-solving and innovation, as it encourages out-of-the-box thinking and synthesizing ideas from different domains.

Encouraging collaboration within and across teams is essential. Collaboration can be fostered through various means, such as shared goals, joint projects, and team-building activities. Moreover, collaboration tools and platforms can facilitate more effective communication and idea-sharing among team members, irrespective of their physical location.

One of the key benefits of cross-functional teams is their ability to make an organization more adaptable to change. Since these teams have a comprehensive understanding of the organization’s functions, they can anticipate the impacts of changes across the board and help to navigate the transition more smoothly. Moreover, because they are not confined to the limited view of a single department, cross- functional teams can often execute change initiatives with a greater appreciation for how each piece of the organization fits into the larger puzzle.

For leaders looking to drive change, investing in developing dedicated cross-functional teams is a powerful strategy. It requires a commitment to reshaping the organizational culture that values and rewards cross-departmental collaboration. By doing so, they lay the foundation for an organization that is more innovative, more cohesive, better aligned, and better prepared for the challenges of the modern business environment.

In previous chapters, we’ve noted that small teams are best. Let’s explore that topic in more depth.

Debunking the myth that large teams are more productive

When organizations attempt to assemble larger teams, they often encounter various challenges that can hinder productivity and effectiveness:

One common issue is communication overload. In larger teams, it becomes increasingly difficult to ensure efficient communication among members. With more voices in the mix, important messages may be lost, leading to misunderstandings and delays.

Another challenge is coordination difficulties. Managing activities and decision-making processes within a large group can be cumbersome. Decision-making becomes slower, and reaching a consensus takes more time. Furthermore, scheduling meetings that accommodate everyone’s availability becomes increasingly challenging.

Social loafing is also a concern in larger teams. Individuals may feel less accountable for their contributions, leading to reduced effort and productivity. Some team members may rely on others to carry the workload, further exacerbating the issue.

While diversity of thought is valuable, larger teams may struggle to align on a common vision or approach. Balancing diverse perspectives with cohesive decision-making becomes crucial to maintain productivity and effectiveness.

Additionally, larger teams may naturally form cliques or subgroups, hindering overall collaboration and creating silos. This can limit the flow of information between different parts of the team and impede progress.

Leadership challenges also arise when leading a large team. Strong management skills are required to ensure that everyone feels heard, motivated, and engaged. Maintaining team morale becomes more complex in a larger group setting.

Resource allocation is another issue to consider in larger teams. Allocating resources such as budget, time, and personnel across a large team can be tricky. Prioritizing projects and managing dependencies becomes essential to avoid inefficiencies.

In summary, while larger teams may bring diverse expertise, they require careful management to mitigate challenges and maintain effectiveness. Striking a balance between team size and collaboration dynamics is essential for optimizing team performance.

Navigating the complexities of collaboration and integration among multiple small teams is paramount for successfully executing large-scale projects, programs, and value streams. That’s the topic we’ll discuss next.

Addressing collaboration and integration challenges in small team environments

Collaboration and synchronization of activities are essential in small team environments for successfully executing large projects or programs. However, integrating the work of multiple small teams can pose significant challenges. Here, we’ll address these challenges and explore strategies for overcoming them effectively:

  • Collaboration: Collaborating effectively among multiple small teams requires clear communication channels, shared goals, and alignment of efforts. Each team must understand its role within the larger project or program and work collaboratively towards common objectives. Regular reviews, meeting events, and collaborative tools can facilitate team communication and coordination. Collaboration isn’t always an intuitive instinct so it should be facilitated and supported intentionally.
  • Synchronization of activities: Synchronizing activities across multiple small teams involves aligning timelines, milestones, and deliverables to ensure smooth progress and avoid bottlenecks. Establishing a common cadence for activities, such as BLAST integrated increment planning sessions and retrospectives, can help teams synchronize their efforts and maintain momentum. Additionally, regular cross-team meetings and stand-ups can provide opportunities to identify dependencies and address any issues promptly. The benefits of this coordination is a considerable factor in managing and eliminating dependencies across teams, products, and capabilities.
  • Team-of-teams working and planning sessions: While multiple teams can come together in a single meeting, that should not be the normal practice for all the reasons previously cited about the challenges of communication and participation in large team settings. Bluntly speaking, large meetings should be limited to information dissemination rather than planning or working sessions. For the most part, each team should appoint one representative to attend a BLAST team-of-teams working or planning session. The ToT representatives form a small group that works in parallel with their assigned teams. This is why BLASTs should not have more than nine teams: to ensure that BLAST representatives can collaborate effectively in small team sessions. If you need more teams, set up additional BLASTs. The assigned representatives can vary across meetings depending on the skills and knowledge required.
  • Integration of work: Integrating the work of multiple small teams requires careful planning and coordination to ensure that individual contributions come together seamlessly to achieve work or product delivery objectives. This involves establishing interfaces and integration points between teams, defining clear standards and guidelines for work integration, and conducting regular integration testing to validate interoperability.
  • Managing dependencies: Managing dependencies between multiple small teams is critical to avoid delays and ensure that project milestones are met. Teams must identify and prioritize dependencies early in the project lifecycle, establish mechanisms for tracking and resolving dependencies, and communicate dependencies transparently across teams. Regular dependency management meetings and dependency mapping exercises can help teams proactively address dependencies and minimize their impact on project timelines.

By addressing these collaboration and integration challenges proactively, organizations can harness the collective capabilities of multiple small teams to deliver integrated increments of new value effectively and efficiently. In summary, effective collaboration, synchronization of activities, integration of work, and management of dependencies are key pillars of success in small team environments, enabling organizations to achieve their strategic objectives and drive business growth. Leveraging data and analytics for decision-making

In the previous chapter, Enhancing Decision-Making in the Lean-Agile Enterprise, we addressed the prevalent issue of organizations being inundated with vast amounts of data. We introduced the BRIA-QIDA (Business-Role-Information-Action and Question-Information- Decision-Action) approach to assist organizations in assessing the necessary information for making informed decisions and executing timely actions. Now, let’s look into the tools and platforms that facilitate the transformation of this data deluge into actionable insights.

In the era of big data, leveraging analytics for strategic decision-making is crucial. Leaders should invest in analytics platforms that provide real-time insights into operational processes, customer behaviors, and market trends. Data-driven decision-making enables organizations to be more agile, responsive, and competitive. Executives should sponsor the BRIA workshops to ensure the development of capabilities that provide immediate access to the information needed to answer our most pressing business questions.

Leveraging data and analytics is fundamental to mastering Lean-Agile principles and effective value stream management practices. In the current technical environment, characterized by large and diverse data sets, harnessing this information becomes a significant competitive advantage.

In Lean-Agile environments, where responsiveness and adaptability are key, data analytics platforms can provide actionable insights rapidly. These insights are crucial for understanding customer needs and behaviors, enabling organizations to deliver value optimally. By closely monitoring and analyzing performance metrics, waste can be minimized through continuous improvement – a core Lean principle.

For VSM, data analytics facilitates a comprehensive view of the value stream, allowing for the identification of bottlenecks and inefficiencies. Real-time data feeds into VSM tools enabling teams to track the flow of value from concept to customer, ensuring that every step in the process contributes positively to the overall outcome. In order to succeed with this approach, data needs to be accessible, organized, high quality, consistent, and relevant. It’s not enough to simply connect your tools to a value stream management platform and trust that insights will arise out of your data. In the 2023 Value Stream Management Consortium’s State of Value Stream Management Report, only 4% of respondents had connected their data to a VSM platform, the lowest of any step in the implementation roadmap.

Leaders should invest in robust analytics platforms and foster a culture where data-driven decision- making is the norm. Executives and employees alike need timely access to the information required to make informed decisions and take appropriate actions. Teams should be empowered to use data insights to guide their work, experiment with new ideas, and pivot quickly based on feedback and changing market conditions.

This data-centric approach aligns with the Agile principle of delivering working solutions frequently. It ensures that decisions are not based on gut feelings or outdated reports but on current, relevant data that reflects the true state of affairs.

In summary, integrating data and analytics into decision-making processes for Lean-Agile and VSM can lead to the following:

  • Improved efficiency and productivity through the elimination of waste
  • Enhanced customer satisfaction by quickly responding to their needs and behaviors
  • Greater market adaptability by understanding and reacting to trends
  • Better risk management by anticipating and mitigating potential issues before they escalate
  • Increased return on investment by making informed strategic decisions that align with the organization’s goals and customer value

For organizations striving to be at the forefront of their industries, the mastery of data and analytics is not just a strategic asset; it’s a necessity.

Implementing VSM tools

Modern VSM tools support DevOps-based software delivery processes by visualizing the flow of work across the end-to-end activities constituting the software delivery value stream and measuring the success of each delivery cycle. In other words, VSM tools help visualize and analyze the flow of materials (for example, code and other assets) and information required to bring a product or service to customers. By identifying bottlenecks, waste, and opportunities for improvement, these tools support Lean and Agile initiatives, enabling better decision-making and prioritization based on customer value.

Through ‘what-if’ analysis, VSM tools are instrumental in optimizing the processes involved in delivering a product or service to customers. These tools play a vital role in visualizing and analyzing value flow. By mapping each step of the product development lifecycle, VSM tools allow teams to identify and understand the value of each action, spotting bottlenecks, redundant tasks, and any form of waste that may exist – be it overproduction, waiting times, unnecessary transportation, over-processing, excess inventory, unnecessary movement, or defects.

Implementing VSM tools is particularly beneficial when used with Lean-Agile initiatives and methodologies. Specifically, VSM tools enhance Lean-Agile solution delivery activities by providing clear insights into how actual work aligns with these principles and how it contributes to the organization’s overall goals.

These data-driven insights enable better decision-making and prioritization of people and functionality. By understanding where value is created and where it is diminished, teams can make informed choices that align their efforts with customer value. Prioritization becomes less about who shouts the loudest and more about objective measures of what will deliver the greatest benefit to the consumer and, consequently, to the business.

In software delivery, as with any other value stream, various metrics are utilized to determine the business value and efficiency of the delivery pipeline:

  • Lead time: The total time it takes from a customer request to the delivery of the final product. It’s a measure of the efficiency of the entire process.
  • Cycle time: This measures the time it takes to complete one activity within the value stream, which helps establish the measurement of performance within each stage.
  • Throughput: The number of units of work a system can deliver in a certain period of time. This metric can reflect a team’s or a process’s capacity.
  • Wait time: The actual time spent waiting between stages of the value stream.
  • Work-in-Progress (WIP): The amount of work that has started but not yet completed. High WIP levels can indicate issues with bottlenecks and overloading of team capacity. High volumes of WIP also tend to hide errors and defects, accumulating more technical debt in the process.
  • Defect rate: The frequency of errors in the deliverables. This can be used to gauge the quality of output and the effectiveness of quality control processes.
  • Value stream flow efficiency: The ratio of process time to lead time indicates how much of the overall cycle time is spent adding value. A low-value stream efficiency indicates significant waste and delays in the value stream steps.
  • Feedback loop speed: The time it takes for information or issues to be communicated to the development team for action, exemplified by the rapid iteration cycle of code deployment, testing, and refinement in software engineering through continuous integration and deployment pipelines.

The successful implementation of VSM tools allows an organization to shift its focus from merely being busy to being productive. It helps create a continuous improvement culture where decision-making is driven by data and customer value, not by flawed assumptions or inertia. By embracing VSM tools, companies can make the necessary strides toward delivering their products and services in the most efficient, effective, and customer-centric way possible.

VSM tools are essential for organizations that aim to optimize value delivery through their software development processes. VSM tools enable these organizations to align around common goals, identify bottlenecks, and improve efficiency. By establishing clear value streams within an organization, VSM tools facilitate the measurement of key DORA metrics such as deployment frequency, lead time for changes, mean time to recovery, and change failure rate, which are crucial for assessing performance and implementing Agile and DevOps methods.

The VSM tools in the market vary widely, and they generally provide capabilities to connect disparate teams and tools, visualize workflows, measure performance metrics, and align organizational structures with customer needs. These tools help break down silos, create a shared understanding of workflow, and automate the collection of performance data to drive continuous improvement.

The authors of this book propose extending the concept of VSM tools to effectively ‘instrument’ all organizational value streams. This concept diverges from workflow systems, which originated from the business process reengineering and improvement fields to instantiate standardized processes. Workflow systems promote a mindset focused on controlling process and information flows, while VSM goes beyond this functionality. VSM not only facilitates process and information flow control but also aids in identifying areas for improvement and experimenting with potential value stream flow enhancements in near real time.

Enhancing software delivery through Flow Metrics

Flow Metrics, coined by Dr. Mik Kersten in the Flow Framework, are pivotal business-level metrics tailored to illuminate the software delivery process. They serve to connect business objectives with IT operations by focusing on the flow of value through product value streams. The key Flow Metrics include:

  1. Flow Velocity: Quantifies the number of work items completed within a specific timeframe, offering insights into team delivery speed and capacity.
  2. Flow Efficiency: Reflects the ratio of active time spent on work items versus the total time taken, highlighting operational efficiency.
  3. Flow Time: Measures the comprehensive duration from work initiation to completion, encompassing both active work and waiting periods.
  4. Flow Load: Indicates the aggregate number of work items in progress at any given moment, pinpointing potential bottlenecks and workload issues.

Though not technically a single metric, Flow Distribution is similarly critical to track as these measures Illustrate the composition of work items (e.g., features, defects) in progress, ensuring a balanced flow of value.

These metrics are indispensable for organizations aiming to refine software delivery processes and harmonize them with business goals. They provide a unified language for business and IT leaders to drive informed decisions and continuous improvement.

While both Flow Metrics and traditional lean-oriented metrics strive to enhance efficiency and value delivery, they diverge in focus and application:

  • Focus and Scope: Flow Metrics are purpose-built for software development, concentrating on value stream flow to bridge business-IT gaps. In contrast, traditional lean metrics, originating from manufacturing, target waste reduction and process enhancement across industries.
  • Key Metrics: Flow Metrics center on metrics like Flow Velocity, Efficiency, Time, Load, and Distribution and are specifically tailored for software delivery contexts. Traditional lean metrics encompass Cycle Time, Lead Time, Throughput, Work In Progress (WIP), and First Pass Yield (FPY), which are applicable broadly across various industry and domain sectors.
  • Application: Flow Metrics are primarily deployed in software development to align IT with business objectives and enhance value delivery. Traditional lean metrics find application in manufacturing and beyond, optimizing processes and reducing waste.
  • Measurement Approach: Flow Metrics emphasize end-to-end value flow, integrating active and idle times to prioritize business value delivery. In contrast, traditional lean metrics target specific process steps for efficiency gains and waste minimization.

In summary, while both metric frameworks aim to optimize efficiency and value delivery, Flow Metrics are tailored to software contexts, emphasizing continuous value flow. Traditional lean metrics, broader in scope, focus on process refinement and waste reduction across industries.

Adopting a change management framework

Organizational change can be disorienting and cause negative disruption if not properly managed. Instead of using an unproven or ad-hoc process, leaders can turn to established change management frameworks to guide their organizations through the turbulence of change. Kotter’s 8-step process for leading change and the ADKAR change model are two of the most prominent and proven frameworks. They support planning, implementing, and sustaining change and are briefly discussed in the following subsections.

Kotter’s 8-step process for leading change

John Kotter, professor emeritus at the Harvard Business School and the founder of Kotter International, a management consulting company, has authored numerous books and articles on leadership and change, including the highly influential book, Leading Change. His seminal works presented the 8-step process for leading change, designed to help organizations overcome the resistance often accompanying significant change and foster acceptance and engagement. Kotter has significantly inspired and impacted how organizations manage and adapt to change, emphasizing the importance of urgency, vision, communication, and making change stick within the organizational culture. Kotter’s contributions have made him one of the leading voices in organizational behavior and leadership studies.

Figure 11.3 illustrates Kotter’s model and is followed by a brief description of each element.

Figure 11.3 – Kotter’s eight-step change model

  1. Create a sense of urgency: Inspire people to act by communicating the need for change that appeals to people’s heads and hearts. This need must be sufficiently compelling to move people out of their comfort zones and escape the inertia of the status quo. It requires open discussions about what’s at stake and the opportunities change presents. For change to succeed, it must inspire and align people to make the change a reality.
  2. Build a guiding coalition: Assemble a volunteer team of effective people from within the organization who can guide, coordinate, and communicate change activities. They must have the power, energy, and influence to support collaborative change.
  3. Form a strategic vision: Craft a vision to direct the change effort and develop strategic initiatives to achieve that vision. Clarify how the future state will differ from the past and get buy-in to make the change a reality through initiatives linked directly to the vision.
  4. Enlist a volunteer army: Significant change can only occur when many people rally around a shared challenge or opportunity. Enlist people who want to actively engage and make a difference. These volunteers must want to collaborate to achieve the goal together.
  5. Enable action by removing barriers: Identify and remove obstacles that hinder the change effort, whether organizational structures, skill deficits, legacy policies, and practices, or anything else that prevents employees from executing the vision.
  6. Generate short-term wins: Plan, define, and execute visible and unambiguous opportunities for success as soon as possible. Short-term wins prove that sacrifices are paying off, which can silence detractors and energize supporters. Ensure wins are real and can be recognized, collected, and widely communicated – early and often – to track progress and energize people to persevere and prevent returning to legacy thinking and acting.
  7. Sustain acceleration: Use the credibility gained from early wins to progressively tackle bigger, more difficult changes. After every win, analyze what went right and needs improving, and then set more ambitious goals. Relentlessly initiate change until the vision becomes a reality, having a bias for action.
  8. Institute change: Anchor the changes into the culture to ensure they become the norm and replace old habits. Communicate and celebrate how the new behaviors and attitudes have improved performance and resulted in wins. Use systems thinking to evaluate systems and processes to avoid expensive and ineffective local optimizations and ensure management practices reinforce the new behaviors, mindsets, and ways of working.

Change initiatives often fail because they don’t address the human element, such as how individuals in the organization feel about the change. Consequently, success with Kotter’s process requires focusing on the people side of change. By following these eight steps, leaders can confidently steer their organizations through the choppy waters of change and emerge stronger and more prepared for the future.

The ADKAR® change model

The ADKAR model, developed by Jeff Hiatt, founder of Prosci in 1994, is a cornerstone of the Prosci method, a comprehensive approach to change management. Hiatt, a former engineer and program manager at Bell Labs, designed this model after studying change management practices across over 700 organizations. ADKAR, an acronym for Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement, was created to address the human side of change and has seen widespread adoption. Hiatt’s contributions to change management extend beyond the ADKAR model, reflecting his extensive research and writing in the field.

ADKAR represents a bottom-up approach, focusing on the transitions individuals need to make for a change to be successful. Figure 11.4 – Prosci ADKAR change model, provides a high-level overview of each component, and a brief description of each element follows.

Figure 11.4 – Prosci ADKAR change model

  • Awareness: Make sure that employees understand the need for change. This means communicating the nature of the change and the rationale behind it.
  • Desire: Encourage a desire to participate in and support the change. This involves engaging with employees on an emotional level and addressing their motivations.
  • Knowledge: Provide the training and education necessary to enable employees to realize the change. This can be through workshops, seminars, or hands-on training.
  • Ability: Give employees the tools, resources, and support they need to implement change effectively. This includes time, budget, and changes to their daily routines.
  • Reinforcement: Implement measures to ensure the change sticks. This includes ongoing support, recognizing success, and embedding change into the organization’s culture.

The ADKAR model is known for its simplicity and effectiveness, allowing leaders to diagnose gaps in their change process and provide targeted interventions at different stages of employee adaptation. It’s a tool that can be used for personal change and enterprise organizational change management initiatives.

ADKAR and Kotter’s model offer two distinct approaches to managing organizational change. Kotter focuses on the strategic aspect of change, prioritizing leadership buy-in and the steps necessary to facilitate effective change management. It starts with establishing a sense of urgency and is followed by creating a guiding coalition, developing a vision and strategy, communicating the change vision, empowering broad-based action, generating short-term wins, consolidating gains, producing more change, and finally, anchoring new approaches in the culture.

In contrast, the ADKAR emphasizes an individual’s journey through change, highlighting key stages of awareness, desire, knowledge, ability, and reinforcement.

Moreover, Kotter’s approach is strategic and top-down, while ADKAR is a bottom-up approach that is more individual-centric, focusing on personal readiness and support for change. Leaders can use these frameworks independently or together to guide their change initiatives, ensuring they are thoughtful, well executed, and ultimately successful.
